# Abstract

This paper uses Semantic Flow Dynamics (SFD) v5.0 (Huang, 2026) as its analytical framework to conduct a complete methodological trial on Chinese civilization. Through a three-step structure—long-duration pattern scanning, xin-model construction, and historical regression testing—seventeen recurrent behavioral patterns and twelve preconditions are extracted from over two thousand years of history, yielding a working model of Chinese civilization's xin (xin-model). The xin-model comprises three components: core xin (there is one and only one center under heaven), power mechanism (the position has no owner; whoever wins takes it), and reproduction mechanism (isomorphic organization). Regression testing against ten historical rupture points confirms the xin-model in all cases, requiring no structural revision; five falsifiability conditions are listed, specifying the death criteria for the xin-model. On this basis, the Accumulation Model is derived, describing the historical rhythm of Chinese civilization: accumulation → sawing-through → rupture → warring → hero → reconstruction → accumulation. Finally, the xin-model is applied to locate the present moment.

**Keywords:** Semantic Flow Dynamics, xin, semantic flow, xin-model, Chinese civilization, civilizational dynamics, Accumulation Model, core xin, positive feedback loop

# Part Zero: Framework and Methodology

## 0.1 Summary of Semantic Flow Dynamics

The analytical framework used in this paper is Semantic Flow Dynamics (SFD) v5.0 (Huang, 2026). The following outlines only the concepts directly invoked in this paper. For the complete framework, see the original document. All concepts marked with UUIDs are cited from SFD v5.0; this paper does not modify their definitions. Concepts independently developed by this paper are defined separately in Section 0.2.

**Primitives.** The framework's primitives are not defined but identified. Individual (__SFD_UUID__.Individual): an identifiable entity. Stimulus (__SFD_UUID__.Stimulus): everything that reaches an individual, which may come from outside (sensory organs, input interfaces) or from inside (bodily states, memory reorganization).

**Definitions.** Xin (__SFD_UUID__.Xin): the consciousness state of an individual. Irreducible, inexhaustible, observable only through its effects. Xin is not a thing inside an individual; it is a description of the individual's state itself. Xin contains layers of different magnitudes: deep (beliefs, core values, changing extremely slowly), middle (knowledge structures, long-term judgment frameworks, changing on the scale of years), shallow (emotions, immediate states, changing on the scale of minutes). Semantic Flow (__SFD_UUID__.SemanticFlow): the ongoing process of xin's continuous change. Semantic flow is not a product; it is a process. Signal (__SFD_UUID__.Signal): a stimulus capable of changing the direction of semantic flow. Determination occurs at the individual's end—the same stimulus may be a signal for one individual and noise for another.

**Supplementary Definitions.** Signal Container (__SFD_UUID__.SignalContainer): a place where signals reside—books, architecture, institutional texts, digital files. Signal containers store and transmit signals but do not produce semantic flow. Channel (__SFD_UUID__.Channel): the path by which a signal travels from one individual to another. Channels carry self-declared signals but do not carry verifiable provenance. Trust (__SFD_UUID__.Trust): the weighting that semantic flow assigns to a specific channel—the same signal, coming from a trusted channel versus an untrusted one, produces completely different changes in the direction of semantic flow.

**Postulates.** The Law of Flux (__SFD_UUID__.FluxLaw): semantic flow operates continuously; there is no moment of stillness. Even when no external signal arrives, semantic flow is operating. The Law of Black-box (__SFD_UUID__.BlackBoxLaw): the next-moment direction of semantic flow cannot be determined by its current direction and incoming signals—no function f exists such that SemanticFlow(t+1) = f(SemanticFlow(t), Signal(t)). This is the framework's core postulate. The Law of Dissipation (__SFD_UUID__.DissipationLaw): semantic flow cannot be reduced to signals. When an individual outputs semantic flow to the outside, it must be encoded as signals, and encoding is necessarily lossy. The Law of Death (__SFD_UUID__.DeathLaw): when an individual perishes, semantic flow cannot be recovered—it cannot be backed up, cannot be rerun, cannot be reproduced in another individual.

**Basic Functions.** The three basic functions are derived from the postulates; they are not independent hypotheses.

Filtering (__SFD_UUID__.Filtering): the current direction of semantic flow determines which stimuli can change it—that is, determines what constitutes a signal and what constitutes noise. Derived from the definition of signal and the Law of Flux. The property of filtering is resistance (__SFD_UUID__.Resistance)—the greater the distance between a stimulus and the current direction of semantic flow, the lower the likelihood that it constitutes a signal. The corollary of filtering is epistemic barrier (__SFD_UUID__.CognitiveBias)—after a signal passes through filtering and changes the direction of semantic flow, the new direction lowers resistance to stimuli in certain directions while simultaneously raising resistance to stimuli in other directions.

Transformation (__SFD_UUID__.Transformation): after a signal passes through filtering and enters semantic flow, the direction of semantic flow changes, but the result of the change is unpredictable. Derived from the Law of Black-box. The same signal entering different semantic flows produces different directional changes.

Collapse (__SFD_UUID__.Collapse): when an individual outputs semantic flow, the semantic flow must be encoded as signals, and encoding is necessarily lossy. Derived from the Law of Dissipation. The property of collapse is expression gap (__SFD_UUID__.ExpressionGap)—semantic flow and the output signal can never fully correspond—and signal distortion (__SFD_UUID__.SignalDistortion)—when an individual's output persistently and observably skews in a particular direction.

**Positive Feedback Loop (__SFD_UUID__.PositiveFeedbackLoop).** Individual A's semantic flow collapses into signals → signals reach Individual B → pass through B's filtering → transform within B's semantic flow → B's semantic flow collapses into signals → signals reach A → the loop begins to run. At the group level: large numbers of individuals operating in similar signal environments produce output signals that reinforce the signal environment; the signal environment shapes the next generation's semantic flow. Positive feedback loops operate across individuals, forming families, societies, civilizations.

**Functional Convergence (__SFD_UUID__.FunctionalConvergence).** The output signal patterns of large numbers of individuals converge—this is an observable fact. But the Law of Black-box guarantees that every individual's semantic flow is different; convergence always has a residual. Functional convergence is the upper limit of what the framework can claim. Derived from the Law of Black-box.

**Instability of Positive Feedback Loops (__SFD_UUID__.Instability).** Positive feedback loops have no permanently sustainable equilibrium. Internal source: the Law of Flux guarantees that semantic flow operates continuously; the Law of Black-box guarantees that directional change is unpredictable; the Law of Death guarantees that individuals are constantly being replaced, and replacement cannot replicate what was lost—residuals within the loop accumulate continuously. External source: everything the loop depends on is changing—signal containers are changing, the environment is changing—when the two change in persistently divergent directions, the gap widens over time. Derived from the Law of Flux, the Law of Black-box, and the Law of Death.

**Generational Replacement (__SFD_UUID__.GenerationalReplacement).** When an individual dies, semantic flow resets to zero and cannot be recovered. The next generation of individuals begins operating in a different signal environment, forming different semantic flows. The continuity of a civilization is not the transmission of semantics (the Law of Black-box and the Law of Dissipation deny this possibility) but the state reproduction of the loop—the next generation forms its own semantic flow within the signal environment left behind by the previous generation. Derived from the Law of Death.

## 0.2 Supplementary Core Concepts

The following concepts are analytical tools developed by this paper on the basis of SFD. Each concept is defined and used only within this paper and does not modify any SFD definition.

**Xin-Model (XinModel).** The framework does not address meta-xin (the initial state of xin is unobservable), but analysis requires a starting point. The xin-model is "our best approximation of xin"—a working model constructed from recurrent behavioral patterns observed over the long duration of history. It is a model, not the true body of xin. The quality of a xin-model is determined by historical regression testing.

**Core Xin Criterion (CoreXinCriterion).** Identifying core xin requires two conditions to be simultaneously satisfied; neither alone suffices.

The first condition: Identity Composition (IdentityComposition). Observe recurrent behavioral tendencies across the long duration of history and extract the identity structure that makes this civilization this civilization. This step must exclude traits shared by all of humanity—all civilizations need food, security, and order; these are human universals, not the identity of any particular civilization. Only tendencies that cannot be shared by other civilizations in comparison are candidates for identity composition.

The second condition: Death Criterion (DeathCriterion). Whether the civilization is willing to die for it—when it is threatened, whether the civilization pays the ultimate price to protect it. Death is the upper limit of price. Willingness to pay the ultimate price indicates that it is not a preference, not a habit, but identity itself. What can be compromised is not core xin; it is garment.

The relationship between the two conditions: the first condition excludes human universals and delimits the candidate range; the second condition excludes preferences and habits, retaining only identity. Using only the first condition would admit important but non-core tendencies (e.g., valuing education—important but compromisable). Using only the second condition would admit emotional reactions (e.g., face—intensely short-term but variable in the long run). When both conditions are simultaneously satisfied, the noise is drained.

Core xin is binary—present or absent. Absent means the civilization is dead. Operational method: look only at structure, not at names. Names keep changing (emperor → chairman → general secretary), but structural isomorphism indicates the same core xin.

**Hero (Hero).** When a gap appears between core xin and reality, the person who attempts alignment. The hero's solution is typically "putting on garments"—wrapping core xin in a shell compatible with reality. In the framework's language: this is the expression gap manifesting at the civilizational level. The more artificial the garment, the greater the expression gap, and the more closure is needed to sustain it.

**Garment (Garment).** The concrete implementation form of core xin—without distinguishing narrative, institution, or organization. Confucianism is a garment, the imperial system is a garment, the civil service examination is a garment, communism is a garment, the party-state system is a garment. Criterion: if it can be replaced without killing core xin, it is a garment. A garment is composed of a set of signal containers: Pravda is a signal container within the garment of communism; legal codes are signal containers within the garment of the imperial system. Garments allow core xin to lower filtering resistance and maintain the operation of the positive feedback loop within the signal environment of a particular era.

**Functional Death (FunctionalDeath).** The permanent rupture of the positive feedback loop constitutes death. Core xin does not naturally attenuate to death due to loop instability—instability produces residual accumulation and container aging, not the disappearance of xin. The death of core xin requires replacement by another xin, or the physical annihilation of the individuals who carry it.

**Silencing (Silencing).** Not an independent mechanism but the observable effect of positive feedback loops and epistemic barriers operating at the institutional level. When a positive feedback loop shapes the direction of all individuals' semantic flow by controlling the signal environment, alternative outputs are not produced at the source—it is not that alternatives were suppressed but that alternatives never arose. Identification criterion for silencing: absence has a fingerprint (traceable exercises of power maintain the absence); inability to conceive has none.

## 0.3 Methodology

This paper adopts a three-step structure:

**Step One: Long-Duration Pattern Scanning.** Scan over two thousand years of history and extract recurrent behavioral patterns. Describe only; do not explain. For each recurrent pattern, append a reverse inquiry: "Why has it never been broken? What preconditions allow it to survive rupture?" Output: pattern inventory (including reverse inquiry results).

**Step Two: Xin-Model Construction.** Take the pattern inventory and use the two conditions of the Core Xin Criterion (identity composition + death criterion) to screen core xin candidate components, merge co-origin patterns, and determine the ordering among components. Output: xin-model.

**Step Three: Historical Regression Testing.** Select rupture points and rerun history using the xin-model. Each rupture point answers four questions: What broke? What did not break (corresponding to which component of the xin-model)? Where did the reconstruction drive come from (can the xin-model explain it)? Where does the xin-model fail to explain? Output: regression test results + xin-model revisions.

After the three-step structure is complete, a dynamical derivation is performed—extracting a dynamical model from the cumulative results of the regression tests.

**Note on Historical Materials.** The historical materials in this paper are based on scholarly consensus; no new claims are made about specific historical facts, and therefore no individual citations are provided. The historical facts cited (such as the Rites Controversy during the Kangxi reign, Zhang Juzheng's Single Whip Reform, Wang Mang's reforms, etc.) are all well-established events verifiable in standard general histories of China. This paper's contribution lies in the structural repositioning of these events, not in the philological examination of the events themselves.

### 0.3.1 Epistemological Honesty Statement on Methodology

The three-step structure of this paper—pattern scanning, xin-model construction, regression testing—has a linear appearance in its presentation, but the actual process is not purely inductive. This point must be stated clearly, rather than allowing readers to mistakenly believe the xin-model "naturally emerged" from the historical materials.

Specifically, the Core Xin Criterion used in Step Two, "xin-model construction," was not inductively derived from the pattern inventory of Step One—it is a screening tool brought in from outside. The two conditions of this criterion (identity composition + death criterion) determine which recurrent patterns are admitted as core xin and which are demoted to garments or dynamical consequences. Without this criterion, the seventeen patterns would not spontaneously assemble into this paper's xin-model; with this criterion, the shape of the xin-model is already partially predetermined.

This is criterion-laden induction, not pure induction. The approach itself is unproblematic—most rigorous theory construction takes this form, because induction with no presuppositions whatsoever does not exist in practice. The issue is whether it is stated clearly.

The criterion adopted by this paper has its theoretical roots: it originates from the analytical perspective provided by the SFD framework, but the criterion itself is this paper's operational tool, not a direct translation of SFD concepts. The first condition (identity composition) asks: Is this behavioral tendency a unique tendency that distinguishes this civilization from others? This is a comparative operation—excluding traits shared by all of humanity, retaining only what makes this civilization this civilization. The second condition (death criterion) asks: When this tendency disappears, does the civilization pay the ultimate price to resist, or does its disappearance signify the death of the civilization itself? This is an observational test—using the upper limit of price (death) to distinguish identity from preference. Together, the Core Xin Criterion is a civilizational analysis tool developed by this paper on the basis of the SFD framework.

This criterion can be challenged: one may argue for a better operationalization, or argue that civilizational identity should be defined by different standards. If you use a different criterion, you will obtain a different xin-model.

Therefore, this paper's xin-model is not "the core xin of Chinese civilization" itself—it is "the working model obtained by applying the SFD framework's Core Xin Criterion in a criterion-laden induction over Chinese historical materials." The quality of this working model is determined by two things: first, whether the criterion itself is sound (a theoretical-level question); second, given the criterion, whether the xin-model passes regression testing (an internal consistency question). This paper primarily addresses the second; the first requires ongoing examination through cross-civilizational extended applications and critical dialogue.

**Operational note for readers.** If you do not accept this criterion, propose a better one, then rerun the process and see whether you can produce a xin-model with greater explanatory power.

## 0.6 Theoretical Positioning

The analytical object of this paper—the long-duration structure of Chinese civilization—has been addressed by multiple theoretical frameworks. The following positions this paper relative to each existing theory, specifying shared observations, divergent claims, and adjudicable predictive differences.

### 0.6.1 Relation to Jin Guantao's Ultra-Stable Structure Hypothesis

Jin Guantao and Liu Qingfeng (1984, 1992, 1993) proposed that Chinese society is an ultra-stable system whose cyclicality arises from the coupling and repair of three sub-structures: economic, political, and ideological. This paper shares the same observational starting point as the ultra-stable structure hypothesis (two thousand years of periodic oscillation and structural repair), and has a direct correspondence at the micro-mechanism level: Jin Guantao's "patrilineal isomorphic unit"—in which the internal structure of the clan is isomorphic to the state structure, serving as a repair template after each dynastic collapse—corresponds to this paper's "reproduction mechanism: isomorphic organization." Priority for this observation belongs to Jin Guantao; this paper's contribution lies not in discovering this mechanism but in its theoretical repositioning.

However, the ontological stances of the two frameworks differ. Jin Guantao treats the economic, political, and ideological as three parallel sub-structures, with cyclicality arising from their coupling and decoupling. This paper's framework is vertical: xin is the sole ontological layer; institutional structure and economic structure are both expressive outcomes produced by xin through positive feedback loops—no peer-level coupling relationship exists. This difference produces an adjudicable predictive divergence: in Jin Guantao's framework, a dramatic change in any one sub-structure can permanently alter the others; in this paper's framework, as long as xin does not change, structural dramatic changes will spring back. The trajectory of China after Reform and Opening—where the economic structure underwent dramatic change from planned economy to market economy, while the political structure sprang back to centralization under Xi Jinping—is more consistent with the latter's prediction.

The deeper divergence lies at the boundary of explanatory power. The ultra-stable structure hypothesis faces two questions that require additional assumptions: first, why did China in the modern era actively dismantle its own coupling structure (abolishing the civil service examination, the imperial system, and the landlord class)? Second, why did the structures rebuilt after decoupling remain isomorphic? This paper's framework provides natural answers to both: because what drives behavior is xin, not structure. When old signal containers can no longer accommodate the demands of core xin, xin drives individuals to actively smash the containers; xin does not disappear with the signal containers, so new containers are shaped by the same xin through the reproduction mechanism into the same form.

### 0.6.2 Relation to Fukuyama's Political Order Theory

Fukuyama (2011, 2014) proposed three pillars of the modern state: a strong state, rule of law, and democratic accountability. He attributed the absence of rule of law to the absence of transcendent religion. This paper's explanatory direction is the reverse: it is not that China lacked transcendent authority, but that core xin does not permit transcendent authority to exist. Transcendent authority implies the existence of an arbiter above the power center, which directly contradicts "there is one and only one center under heaven."

The Rites Controversy during the Kangxi reign is the clearest case. Matteo Ricci's strategy of "accommodating Confucianism" was standard "putting on garments"—foreign signals wrapping themselves in local garments to lower filtering resistance. The turning point came when Pope Clement XI in 1715 demanded that Chinese converts pledge allegiance to a papal authority above the emperor. At that moment, Catholicism transformed from "a foreign signal wearing garments" to "an independent node claiming transcendent status." The epistemic barrier shaped by core xin activated precisely—the signal direction directly contradicted core xin, and resistance was maximized. The wording of the prohibition decree is revealing: the problem was not the doctrinal content of Catholicism but the pope's claim to an authority above the emperor. This matches this paper's prediction and does not match Fukuyama's prediction (that transcendent religion failed to grow due to unsuitable soil). The problem was not the soil; it was the epistemic barrier.

### 0.6.3 Relation to Toynbee's Civilizational Life-Cycle Theory

Toynbee (1934–1961) used a biological-organism metaphor to describe civilizations: civilizations have natural cycles of growth, maturity, aging, and death. This paper does not adopt this metaphor. Positive feedback loops are not biological organisms; they do not naturally attenuate to death. Civilizational death has two mechanisms: the physical annihilation of the individuals who carry xin (the Inca, the Aztecs), or the permanent replacement of the core xin's positive feedback loop by another loop (the replacement of Roman civic religion by Christianity). There is no third mechanism of "natural death from old age." Positive feedback loops have instability (derived from the Law of Flux, the Law of Black-box, and the Law of Death), but instability produces residual accumulation and container aging, not the demise of the loop itself—demise requires external replacement.

### 0.6.4 Relation to Ray Huang's Macro-History

Ray Huang (1981, 1988) pointed out that traditional Chinese governance lacked the capacity for numerically managed administration. This paper repositions this observation as core xin's tool selection, not an independent causal variable. Moral governance was selected because it does not generate independent nodes—the ultimate interpretive authority over morality lies with the emperor. Rule of law and precision administration are the opposite; both require independent interpretive or auditing authority, both triggering the epistemic barrier shaped by core xin—the signal direction points toward "there is an arbiter above the center," directly contradicting core xin, and resistance is maximized. The abolition of Zhang Juzheng's Single Whip Reform is the clearest case: the reform did not trigger the epistemic barrier while Zhang Juzheng was alive (because the reform's signals came from the center itself); once he died, the reform became an ownerless independent system, and the epistemic barrier activated immediately.

### 0.6.5 Relation to Zhao Dingxin's Confucian-Legalist State Theory

Zhao Dingxin (2015) positioned Confucian-Legalist complementarity as the fundamental structure of Chinese history. This paper demotes it to garment. The criterion is the two conditions of the Core Xin Criterion: during the Mao era, the Confucian-Legalist system was comprehensively abolished, yet the structure of the power center not only did not disappear but reached its historical peak—the disappearance of Confucian-Legalism did not trigger a civilizational-level rupture of the positive feedback loop (failing the death criterion), and Confucian-Legalism itself is not an identity composition unique to China (Japan also has Confucian-Legalist elements, failing the identity composition condition). Core xin, when facing structural challenges (the republican experiment, the democracy movement), triggered intense epistemic barrier reactions every time—simultaneously satisfying both conditions. What triggers an epistemic barrier reaction is core xin; what does not is garment.

### 0.6.6 Relation to Weber's Sociology of Domination

Weber (1922) distinguished three types of legitimate domination: traditional, charismatic, and legal-rational. This paper argues that Weber's framing presupposition does not hold in the Chinese context. Weber presupposes that legitimacy is an authorization mechanism independent of the fact of power. China's power mechanism (the position has no owner; whoever wins takes it) eliminates this intermediary layer: victory itself is legitimacy; no external authorization is needed. Weber's three types appeared in Chinese history, but as post-hoc narrative decoration (garment), not as ex-ante authorization sources.

### 0.6.7 Methodological Note

The long-duration pattern scanning of this paper shares the "looking past events to see structure" orientation with the Annales School's longue durée (Braudel, 1949) in methodology, but the objects of attention differ: Braudel's long duration is the slow movement of material conditions and geography; this paper's long duration is the recurrence of behavioral patterns—whose stability comes from the continuous reproduction of the deep layer of xin through positive feedback loops, not from the material environment.

### 0.6.8 Intra-Framework Clarifications

To prevent misreadings, three structural relationships that might be perceived as tensions are clarified here.

First, "xin is the sole ontological layer" versus "if the reproduction mechanism is destroyed, xin dies." Xin determines the shape of structure, but xin depends on positive feedback loops to persist—loops require signal containers and individuals. The causal direction of shape is unidirectional (xin → structure), but the conditions of persistence are bidirectionally dependent.

Second, "legitimacy is winning" versus "heroes need to put on garments." Legitimacy is acquired through winning (the moment of victory needs no narrative); legitimacy is maintained through garments (long-term rule requires tools that lower filtering resistance). The two operate at different points in time and do not constitute a contradiction.

Third, "core xin does not naturally attenuate" versus "garments are getting thinner." The positive feedback loop of core xin operates continuously (Law of Flux), but the loop has instability—what changes is not core xin itself but the external signal environment. Garments getting thinner does not mean core xin is weakening; it means the materials available for wrapping core xin are being eliminated one by one by the signal environment of industrial civilization.

## 0.7 Falsifiability

The xin-model is a model, not a creed. The following lists the evidence that can kill it.

**Evidence that could overturn core xin:**

(1) A stable multi-center political structure appeared in Chinese history (not maintained by external force, persisting for more than one generation) that did not presuppose unification as its default objective. The strongest known counter-example candidate is the long coexistence of the Southern Song and the Jin (1127–1234), but the Southern Song consistently maintained "recovering the Central Plains" as its official narrative; coexistence was a military reality, not a cognitive acceptance, and does not constitute a counter-example.

(2) After some rupture, the rebuilt structure was not isomorphic to core xin—for example, a stable federation, city-state system, or any non-single-center structure was rebuilt—and this structure was not a transitional state but a new normal.

**Evidence that could overturn the power mechanism:**

(3) A non-transferable source of legitimacy appeared in Chinese history—some transcendent authority that successfully and durably constrained the logic of "whoever wins takes it."

**Evidence that could overturn the reproduction mechanism:**

(4) After some rupture, all isomorphic organizations were thoroughly destroyed, yet core xin still survived and drove reconstruction.

**Evidence that could challenge the framework's discriminating power:**

(5) The xin-model produced by applying the same method to another civilization (such as Iran, Japan, or India) is isomorphic to that of China.

**Note on "all 10 cases confirmed."** The regression test results show that the xin-model requires no structural revision at any rupture point, but this does not mean the xin-model is perfect. Each rupture point records "where the xin-model fails to explain," and these unexplained residuals are not decorative self-deprecation but honest markers of the framework's boundaries. A model's confirmation in all known cases demonstrates only internal consistency, not external validity—external validity requires ongoing testing against the falsifiability conditions above, as well as cross-civilizational extended applications.

# Part One: Constructing the Xin-Model

## 1.1 Two-Thousand-Year Pattern Scanning

**Scanning range:** Shang–Zhou → Qin–Han → Wei–Jin–Northern and Southern Dynasties → Sui–Tang → Song–Yuan → Ming–Qing

**Methodological reminder: describe only, do not explain.** Each item below is a behavioral pattern repeatedly observed in history, not a causal attribution.

### A. Recurrent Patterns Extracted from Rupture Points

### A1. Power Structure Patterns

**Pattern 1: After every regime change, the victor rebuilds a single power center.** Qin unification → emperor system. Han overthrows Qin → Han inherits Qin's system. Sui unification → replicates the Han system. Tang overthrows Sui → Tang inherits Sui's system. Song unification → centralizes military authority. Yuan → khan/emperor. Ming → abolishes the chancellery; power concentration becomes more extreme. Qing → continues the Ming system. 1949 → single power center. Not once after a regime change has a stable multi-center structure appeared.

**Pattern 2: Rebels replicate rather than replace the power structure.** Chen Sheng and Wu Guang proclaimed themselves kings. Liu Bang proclaimed himself emperor. Huang Chao established a state and proclaimed himself emperor. Zhu Yuanzhang founded the Ming. Li Zicheng founded the Great Shun and proclaimed himself emperor. Hong Xiuquan proclaimed himself Heavenly King and established Tianjing—even when introducing a foreign religious framework, the imperial structure was still replicated. The boundary of rebels' imagination is "put someone else in that seat," not "dismantle that seat."

**Pattern 3: The lower classes challenge the legitimacy of power but not its structure.** The discursive pattern of peasant uprisings: "The blue sky is dead; the yellow sky shall rise," "Equal land and no taxes," "Acting on behalf of heaven"—all pointing toward "this emperor is no good," not "there should be no emperor."

**Pattern 4: The power center tends to annihilate or co-opt all independent power nodes.** Qin annihilated the aristocracies of the six states. Early Han reduced the feudal domains. Song's "releasing military authority over cups of wine." Ming abolished the chancellery, established the Embroidered Uniform Guard and the Eastern Depot. Qing established the Grand Council. Religious forces never formed a durable power node independent of imperial authority in China.

**Pattern 5: The intellectual class attaches to the power center rather than operating independently.** The Hundred Schools of pre-Qin → Qin's burning of books and burying of scholars → Han's exclusive elevation of Confucianism. The civil service examination locked the intellectual class's path of advancement to the power center.

### A2. Cyclical Patterns

**Pattern 6: The cycle of order → exploitation → endurance → explosion → rebuilding the same order.** The trigger for each cycle differs, but the structure of the cycle is the same: container aging → bottom-level pressure accumulates → explosion → warring → a new single center rebuilt.

**Pattern 7: After each rebuilding, the new regime attempts to patch the specific causes of the previous collapse without changing the structure.** Han drew lessons from Qin's tyranny → reduced taxes and corvée, but did not change the emperor system. Song drew lessons from the late Tang's regional warlords → centralized military authority, but did not change centralized governance.

### A3. Legitimacy Patterns

**Pattern 8: Political legitimacy ultimately derives from factual possession, not from bloodline or divine mandate.** Liu Bang: a commoner. Zhu Yuanzhang: a beggar. Foreigners (Yuan, Qing) could also take the seat—the legitimacy threshold is "you won," not "who you are."

**Pattern 9: Economic prosperity is treated as evidence of the power center's legitimacy.** The Rule of Wen and Jing, the Prosperity of Zhenguan, the Kangxi–Qianlong Golden Age—the economy is never independently assessed; it is always nested within the narrative of power legitimacy.

**Pattern 10: The "Mandate of Heaven" as a transferable certificate of legitimacy.** The Mandate of Heaven is not bound to a specific bloodline—it can be "revoked." Unlike the European "divine right of kings": the Mandate of Heaven is transferable; you can seize it, and once seized, the Mandate is yours.

### A4. Cultural and Assimilation Patterns

**Pattern 11: After foreign conquest, the conquerors are assimilated by China's positive feedback loop, not the reverse.** Northern Wei Emperor Xiaowen's sinification. Liao and Jin established Han-style bureaucracies. Qing comprehensively adopted the Ming system. The direction of assimilation is unidirectional—after conquerors enter China's signal environment, through filtering and transformation, the direction of their semantic flow converges toward the existing loop (functional convergence). The only exception is the Mongols—the Yuan dynasty retained the most steppe institutions, but it was also the shortest-lived unified dynasty (97 years).

**Pattern 12: The unified writing system continues to operate during periods of regime rupture.** After the unification of writing (Qin), regardless of how regimes fragmented, the writing system remained unbroken.

**Pattern 13: The persistent presence of the concept of orthodoxy—even during periods of fragmentation, all parties claim to be the sole legitimate successor.** "Orthodoxy" means "there should be only one center under heaven." Fragmentation is viewed as pathological; unification is viewed as normal.

### A5. Social Structure Patterns

**Pattern 14: The persistence of clan/lineage as the basic organizational unit.** The internal structure of the clan replicates the structure of the power center: patriarchal authority, hierarchical ordering by seniority, clan rules and clan law. Micro-structure is isomorphic to macro-structure.

**Pattern 15: The geographic imagination of Great Unity persists.** The geographic concepts of "all under heaven," "the Nine Provinces," and "China" do not disappear during periods of fragmentation. After every unification, the new regime attempts to restore and even expand the territorial extent of the previous dynasty.

**Pattern 16: Agricultural economic structure supports the power center pattern.** For two thousand years, China's economic foundation has consistently been agriculture, and agriculture requires large-scale hydraulic engineering and land management.

### A6. Closure and Expression Gap Patterns

**Pattern 17: The garments that successive heroes dress core xin in grow thinner over time.** Qin–Han Legalism/Confucianism was highly compatible with core xin. Sui–Tang Buddhism was restricted to non-political roles. Song–Ming Neo-Confucianism strengthened moral argumentation, but imperial power was already naked. The Qing's Manchu–Han dual-track system had visible structural seams. Mao's communism was structurally entirely mismatched with the inner core. From Deng to the present, even the pretense of garments cannot be sustained. Trend: the inventory of available garments is shrinking; the range of choices is narrowing.

### B. Reverse Inquiry—What Preconditions Prevent These Patterns from Breaking?

**B1. Why do the power structure patterns (Patterns 1–5) never break?**

**Precondition 1: The presupposition that "the power center can be contested."** Never denied since Chen Sheng and Wu Guang. Every successful change of dynasty reinforces this presupposition—the positive feedback loop is continuously strengthened.

**Precondition 2: The presupposition that "the position has no owner."** The transferability of the Mandate of Heaven structurally guarantees this.

**Precondition 3: No durable alternative structure has ever appeared.** Every force that might have evolved into an alternative structure was annihilated or co-opted. The civil service examination locked all talented individuals' paths to "becoming an official"; the exclusive elevation of Confucianism locked the direction of knowledge to the classics; the burning of books directly eliminated alternative texts. Behind every absence lies a traceable exercise of power—absence has a fingerprint.

**Precondition 4: Knowledge production is bound to the power structure.** The deep effect of the civil service examination is silencing: all capable thinkers are channeled into the same pathway; core xin shapes the direction of all intellectuals' semantic flow by controlling the signal environment, and alternative thinking is never produced at the source.

**B2. Why do the cyclical patterns (Patterns 6–7) never break?**

**Precondition 5: The rebuilt structure is identical to the collapsed structure.** Because rebels have no alternative; they can only replicate—the template provided by the reproduction mechanism is isomorphic organization.

**Precondition 6: The periodicity of land concentration is synchronized with the power structure.** The economic cycle is bound to the political cycle.

**B3. Why do the legitimacy patterns (Patterns 8–10) never break?**

**Precondition 7: No transcendent source of legitimacy exists.** The Mandate of Heaven is transferable → dynastic change is always structurally legitimate. Because the epistemic barrier shaped by core xin prevents the establishment of any transcendent authority.

**Precondition 8: The economy has never acquired an evaluative system independent of politics.** Economic crises automatically convert into political crises.

**B4. Why do the cultural and assimilation patterns (Patterns 11–13) never break?**

**Precondition 9: The writing system and canonical texts constitute a basis of continuity across regimes.** Signal containers of the positive feedback loop survive regime ruptures.

**Precondition 10: The concept of "orthodoxy" renders fragmentation cognitively unacceptable.** Because the epistemic barrier shaped by core xin through filtering—the signal direction of multi-centeredness is the farthest from core xin, and resistance is maximal.

**B5. Why do the social structure patterns (Patterns 14–16) never break?**

**Precondition 11: Clan structures survive every regime rupture and internally replicate the isomorphic structure of the power center.** This is the reproduction of the positive feedback loop at the micro level—its most enduring link.

**Precondition 12: The agricultural economy's demand for large-scale coordination provides the material basis for the power center.**

**Scanning output: 17 recurrent patterns + 12 preconditions.**

**Patterns 1, 4, 5, 13, and 15 are different facets of the same thing.** Common structure: there is one and only one center under heaven; everything else orbits it.

**Patterns 2, 3, and 7 are by-products of core xin's overwhelming strength.** Everyone with power agrees on this structure alone. Alternatives are not suppressed; they are silenced. Subsumed under core xin.

### The Xin-Model

After merging, three components:

**Core Xin (CoreXin): There is one and only one center under heaven.** The correct state of the world is a single center governing all. This is not a policy preference but a default value—a direction determined by the deep layer of xin. Core Xin Criterion verification: identity composition—Europe, Japan, and India do not have this default; death criterion—every time this structure has been challenged in history, the civilization has paid the ultimate price to restore it. Covers Patterns 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 13, 15.

**Power Mechanism (PowerMechanism): The position has no owner; whoever wins takes it.** That sole center belongs to no person, bloodline, or deity. Legitimacy derives from victory itself. Core Xin Criterion verification: identity composition—Europe's divine right of kings and Japan's unbroken imperial line are not this structure; death criterion—the transferability of the Mandate of Heaven has never been relinquished. Covers Patterns 8, 10.

**Reproduction Mechanism (ReproductionMechanism): Isomorphic Organization.** All micro-organizational forms whose internal structure replicates that of core xin. Historically, the clan has been the most enduring carrier, but not the only one. Academic cliques, political factions, party organizations, the military—any organization whose internal structure is "one center governing everyone" is performing the micro-level reproduction function of the positive feedback loop. Core Xin Criterion verification: identity composition—the degree of clan isomorphism in China is unique in civilizational comparison; death criterion—if the reproduction mechanism is thoroughly destroyed, core xin cannot persist. Covers Pattern 14.

**Ordering:** Core xin > power mechanism > reproduction mechanism.

# Part Two: Historical Regression Testing

Ten rupture points are used to test the xin-model. Each rupture point answers four questions: What broke? What did not break? Where did the reconstruction drive come from? Where does the xin-model fail to explain?

Three rupture points (Northern and Southern Dynasties → Sui unification, Xinhai Revolution → 1949, Reform and Opening → Xi Jinping) receive in-depth analysis. The remaining rupture points are presented in skeletal format.

## 2.1 The Qin Unification (221 BCE)

**What broke:** Feudalism, multi-center legitimacy, an independent intellectual ecology (the Hundred Schools).

**What did not break:** Core xin existed before the Qin unification. The Zhou-era concept of "all under heaven" already presupposed a single center. Qin did not invent core xin; it was the first to install core xin in a matching signal container. The commandery-county system was core xin's first well-fitting garment.

**Reconstruction drive:** Initial construction, not reconstruction. Core xin provided direction, the power mechanism provided fuel, and the reproduction mechanism provided the template.

**Where the xin-model fails to explain:** Why Qin and not another state (framework boundary—the Law of Black-box seals off the specific choices of individuals). Why Qin collapsed in only fifteen years—possible regularity: when core xin first acquires a complete signal container, it tends toward overexpression ("first-build overshoot").

## 2.2 Wang Mang / Xin Dynasty (8–23 CE)

**What broke:** The Western Han container; the stable operation of the container.

**What did not break:** Core xin was entirely intact—Wang Mang's usurpation of the Han was itself an expression of core xin. The power mechanism was intact. The reproduction mechanism was intact.

**Reconstruction drive:** "Restoring the Han dynasty" was the most readily available direction; the power mechanism drove heroes to rise; powerful clans provided the social base.

**Where the xin-model fails to explain:** The inertia of an existing positive feedback loop provides a legitimacy discount—flying an old banner has lower filtering resistance than a new banner. The tension between core xin and the reproduction mechanism appeared for the first time—the Eastern Han's powerful clans were simultaneously the reproduction mechanism and independent nodes.

## 2.3 Northern and Southern Dynasties → Sui Unification (316–589 CE) — In-Depth Analysis

This is the xin-model's most severe stress test. Three hundred years of fragmentation, no unified regime, and northern China repeatedly occupied by non-Han regimes. If core xin can survive under these conditions, its resilience cannot be explained by any particular institution or cultural tradition—it must be something more fundamental than institutions and culture.

### What Broke

The unified regime container shattered completely. After the fall of the Western Jin (316 CE), China entered one of the longest periods of fragmentation in human history. The north cycled through at least sixteen regimes over three hundred years, most established by non-Han peoples. The Han monopoly on the power center was broken. Central control over localities dropped to near zero. The economic base was repeatedly destroyed.

### What Did Not Break — Three Pieces of Evidence for Core Xin's Survival

**First, every regime claimed sole orthodoxy.** Throughout three hundred years, neither Han nor non-Han regimes ever proposed a political plan of "we can coexist with the other side." Fu Jian of the Former Qin (Di ethnicity), upon unifying the north, immediately launched a southern campaign, motivated by the need to complete "the unification of all under heaven"—a Di monarch executing core xin's directive. The direction of his semantic flow had already been shaped by China's positive feedback loop. Emperor Xiaowen of the Northern Wei (Xianbei ethnicity) systematically dismantled the Xianbei's alternative structures: banning the Xianbei language, banning Xianbei dress, adopting Han surnames, relocating the capital to Luoyang. Because if you want to rule China, you must wear the garments that core xin demands.

**Second, the power mechanism was extremely active throughout the three hundred years.** The frequency of regime change reached its historical peak. Every change was an expression of the power mechanism: the position has no owner; whoever wins takes it. Moreover, non-Han peoples could also take the seat—this precisely verifies the "ownerless" property of the power mechanism.

**Third, the reproduction mechanism automatically filled the vacuum when regimes were absent.** When the power structure at the national level collapsed, core xin's positive feedback loop did not stop—it descended to the micro level, where isomorphic organizations continued to execute it. In the south, the great aristocratic clans were the micro-carriers of core xin. In the north, the internal structure of the fortified communities (wubao) was strikingly isomorphic: one wubao master governing everything. The wubao masters spontaneously established centralized communities because core xin was their default value—the deep layer of xin, through the reproduction mechanism, continued to operate.

### Reconstruction Drive

Core xin provided direction, the power mechanism provided fuel, and the reproduction mechanism provided the template. The sinification of the Northern Wei played a critical bridging role—it narrowed the divergence in semantic flow directions between north and south (functional convergence), providing the foundation for eventual unification.

### Where the Xin-Model Fails to Explain

Why did the fragmentation last three hundred years rather than a shorter period? The dynamical details of functional convergence—different conquerors integrated into China's positive feedback loop at different speeds, seemingly related to the scale and stability of the conquerors' own positive feedback loops: the Xianbei (fully integrated), the Mongols (partially integrated, 97-year short life). Preliminary regularity: the more stable and larger the conqueror's own positive feedback loop, the less complete the integration and the less stable the containers established. However, "the stability of a positive feedback loop" currently has no measurement method independent of outcomes—this is a circularity risk in the framework that needs to be resolved in future cross-civilizational applications.

## 2.4 Song → Yuan (1279)

**What broke:** The Han-led regime, the continuous operation of the civil service examination, the political status of Han scholar-officials.

**What did not break:** Core xin encountered a collision of two positive feedback loops, but China's core xin was not replaced—Mongol on the surface, Han institutions underneath. After the fall of the Yuan, the Ming restored the Han system.

**Reconstruction drive:** "Expel the Tartars, restore China" was the default direction; the Red Turban uprisings released the power mechanism; clans and local organizations preserved the institutional template.

## 2.5 Ming → Qing → Taiping Heavenly Kingdom (1644–1864)

**What broke:** Ming → Qing: the Ming dynasty's regime container. Taiping Heavenly Kingdom: Qing control over the middle and lower Yangtze.

**What did not break:** The Qing comprehensively adopted the Ming system—verifying the conqueror integration regularity. The Taiping Heavenly Kingdom introduced a foreign signal system (a Christian variant) but actually built an imperial structure—an extreme demonstration of functional convergence of the positive feedback loop: even when signals of an entirely different direction are introduced, after filtering and transformation, the collapse output of semantic flow remains isomorphic to core xin.

**Reconstruction drive:** The Qing was not a reconstruction but a direct takeover. Post-Taiping reconstruction relied on local militia (the Xiang Army, the Huai Army)—a militarized expression of the reproduction mechanism.

## 2.6 The Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895)

**What broke:** The credibility of the "Chinese substance, Western application" garment; the tributary system; the "all-under-heaven order" cognitive framework.

**What did not break:** Core xin was entirely intact—the shock hit the garment, not the bone.

**Where the xin-model fails to explain:** Why the shock of the Sino-Japanese War was greater than that of the Opium Wars—filtering resistance may be related to the comparability of the signal source. Japan was more "comparable" than Britain (both East Asian), so it penetrated resistance that the Opium Wars could not. This observation exceeds SFD v5.0's current description of resistance (greater distance means greater resistance) and may require further refinement in future framework versions.

## 2.7 The Hundred Days' Reform (1898)

**What broke:** The path of top-down reform.

**What did not break:** Core xin was intact—Empress Dowager Cixi's coup was an epistemic barrier reaction of core xin. Constitutional monarchy meant constraining the center; core xin demands that the center be unconstrained—the signal direction was maximally distant from core xin, and resistance was maximized.

**Where the xin-model fails to explain:** The success of the Meiji Restoration suggests: Japan's core xin was bound to a person (the emperor); China's was bound to a structure—this structural difference determined the fundamental divergence in modernization paths.

## 2.8 Xinhai Revolution → Communist Founding (1911–1949) — In-Depth Analysis

This is the most complete demonstration of the Accumulation Model in a single cycle. Thirty-eight years, from the collapse of the imperial system to the reconstruction of a new single center, with the full six steps of accumulation → sawing-through → rupture → warring → hero → reconstruction all visible here.

### What Broke

The imperial system—core xin's most stable garment for two thousand years—shattered. The civil service examination was abolished in 1905. Two of core xin's main garments of two thousand years disappeared within six years. The problem: there was nothing suitable in the garment closet.

### Thirty-Eight Years of Searching for Garments

**First garment: The Republic (1912–1916).** The republic required multi-center coexistence, directly contradicting core xin. Yuan Shikai hollowed out the republic almost immediately, and in 1915 proclaimed himself emperor outright. Yuan Shikai's self-proclamation was not a personal choice by an ambitious man; it was the natural expression of core xin—the republic's multi-center structure did not match the default value, and the positive feedback loop automatically sprang back to a single center.

**Second garment: "Local strongmen" during the warlord period (1916–1928).** This was not a garment but garment fragments. Each warlord built a micro-centralized regime within his own territory—verifying Pattern 2 and the reproduction mechanism.

**Third garment: The Nationalist Party's party-state system (1928–1949).** Chiang Kai-shek replaced "emperor" with "party." Why did this garment also fail to hold? Two reasons: first, organizational penetration was far weaker than the Communist Party's, incapable of controlling "everything." Second, the Japanese invasion created a power vacuum, and the Communist Party established a parallel regime in the countryside.

**Fourth garment: Communism (1949–).** Communism was structurally astonishingly compatible with core xin: one party leading everything (the sole center), the party replacing the Mandate of Heaven as the source of legitimacy, land reform destroying old independent nodes, the work-unit system binding every individual within organizations (comprehensive coverage by the reproduction mechanism). But this garment had a fatal structural problem: it was false. The original version of communism presupposed the withering away of the state and the dispersal of power—precisely the opposite of core xin.

### The Hero

Mao Zedong precisely executed the function of the hero: he emerged victorious from the warring, found a garment, and enabled core xin to survive in the signal environment of industrial civilization. But the definition of hero does not include moral judgment. "Hero" in the framework is functional—performing the function of "aligning core xin with reality."

### Where the Xin-Model Fails to Explain

Why communism rather than fascism or another ideology? The specific selection of garment involves historical contingency—the Law of Black-box seals off the specific decisions at the individual level. The framework can only explain the structural conditions a garment must satisfy (it must be compatible with a single center), not why this garment and not that one. Why was the thirty-eight-year warring period so long? It may be positively correlated with "the difficulty of finding garments"—after the imperial system was scrapped, a new garment had to be invented.

## 2.9 Reform and Opening → Xi Jinping (1978–Present) — In-Depth Analysis

This is a live stress test of the xin-model. Readers can directly compare it against present reality to judge the xin-model's explanatory power.

### What Broke

The substantive content of the communist garment was hollowed out. Reform and Opening retained the name of communism but gutted nearly all of its substance: the planned economy became a market economy, public ownership became predominantly private ownership, and class-struggle discourse disappeared. The comprehensive coverage of the work-unit system was broken.

### What Did Not Break — Triple Verification of Core Xin

**First verification: Reform was strictly confined to the economic domain; the political structure was not touched.** Deng Xiaoping's "Four Cardinal Principles" were essentially saying: garments can be changed; bones cannot be touched. The leadership of the Communist Party (the sole center) is non-negotiable.

**Second verification: Tiananmen, 1989.** The demands of the Tiananmen movement—freedom of the press, accountability, dialogue—were fatal at the level of core xin. Freedom of the press meant signal channels escaping the control of the power center. Accountability meant the existence of an arbiter above the power center. Core xin's epistemic barrier reaction activated. The crackdown was not one person's decision—the entire power structure rapidly fell into line after the crackdown, indicating that the epistemic barrier reaction originated from core xin's default values, permeating the entire system through the reproduction mechanism.

**Third verification: Xi Jinping's centralization.** Reform and Opening produced independent nodes (private entrepreneurs, the internet, the middle class). Core xin drove the annihilation of independent nodes: Xi's anti-corruption campaign systematically eliminated independent power bases within the party; private enterprises were required to establish party organizations; the internet was brought under comprehensive surveillance. The abolition of presidential term limits (2018) was a landmark event: removing constraints that had only been pretended.

### Structural Description of the Present Position

**Phase: Normal state (accumulation).**

**Garment status: Running bare.** The communist garment exists in name only. Economic performance is the only fig leaf, but this is a historically unprecedented state—historically, legitimacy has never rested on economics alone.

**Sawing status:** Two saw lines are cutting simultaneously. The first—container aging: the instability of the positive feedback loop continuously accumulates residuals (derived from the Law of Flux, the Law of Black-box, and the Law of Death). The second—structural friction: the market economy continuously produces independent nodes; suppress one batch, and new ones grow.

**An unprecedented variable: the signal environment of industrial civilization.** The core operating logic of industrial civilization—market economy, information flow, rule of law, individual rights—every single one structurally contradicts core xin. Core xin's epistemic barrier maintains maximal resistance to these signals, but complete closure means withdrawing from industrial civilization (the North Korea model), and China's economic scale makes this infeasible.

## 2.10 Regression Test Summary

**The xin-model holds at all 10 rupture points with no structural revision.**

Candidates for upgrade to formal supplements of the xin-model: (1) The relationship between the speed of functional convergence and the stability of the conqueror's own positive feedback loop (three cases supporting). (2) The structural tension between core xin and the reproduction mechanism (five cases confirming). (3) Filtering resistance as a diagnostic tool for the success or failure of institutional reform (three cases supporting).

Requiring further research: the spectrum of execution strategies for Pattern 4, the legitimacy discount provided by the inertia of existing loops, the relationship between the length of warring periods and the difficulty of finding garments, the relationship between resistance and the comparability of signal sources, and the scale effects of modern silencing.

Beyond the framework's boundaries: whether industrial civilization signals can ultimately penetrate the epistemic barrier, how long "economic openness + political closure" can hold, and the sustainability of core xin running bare.

# Part Three: Dynamical Derivation

## 3.1 The Accumulation Model (AccumulationModel)

Ten rounds of regression testing reveal a recurrent dynamical process. The theoretical basis of the following model is the instability of positive feedback loops (derived from the Law of Flux, the Law of Black-box, and the Law of Death): positive feedback loops have no permanently sustainable equilibrium; internal residuals accumulate continuously, and external gaps widen continuously. The Accumulation Model is the specific pattern that emerges when this general instability runs under the particular xin-model conditions of Chinese civilization.

**Normal State / Accumulation.** Core xin is fully operative; the positive feedback loop is stable. Surface stability, subsurface accumulation. Two lines are sawing simultaneously: container aging (bureaucratic corruption, resource concentration, bottom-level pressure accumulation, rising maintenance costs—the internal source of instability); garment drift (each generation's semantic flow is continuously changing [Law of Flux], and the gap between the claims of signal containers and the signals individuals actually receive widens—the external source of instability).

**Sawing-Through.** The power mechanism repeatedly impacts the container. Most impacts fail, but each one consumes the container's resources. The final successful impact is not because it was particularly strong but because all the preceding failed impacts had already sawed the container down to a single layer of skin. This mechanism explains why so many dynasties fell to seemingly trivial causes.

**Rupture.** The container shatters. The power mechanism is fully released. Core xin has not broken. The reproduction mechanism continues to operate at the grassroots level.

**Warring.** Everyone competes for the sole position. Each competitor builds a micro-centralized regime—the reproduction mechanism operating at the micro level. The length of the warring period is positively correlated with "the difficulty of finding garments."

**Hero Phase.** The victor emerges from the warring. Function: find a garment that allows core xin to survive in the present reality. Legitimacy derives from victory (power mechanism). Absence of a hero = prolonged chaos.

**Reconstruction.** The hero puts on the garment and rebuilds the structure demanded by core xin. The reproduction mechanism plugs into new signal containers. The power mechanism is pressed back into dormancy. The positive feedback loop restarts, and instability begins accumulating anew.

**Complete expression: Accumulation → Sawing-Through → Rupture → Warring → Hero → Reconstruction → Accumulation**

## 3.2 Present Position

**Phase: Normal state (accumulation).** The specifics have been expanded in the in-depth analysis of Section 2.9; here only a structural summary is provided.

**Garment status: Running bare.** The communist garment has been hollowed out; the economic-performance fig leaf is peeling off; core xin expresses itself directly.

**Structural predictions (non-temporal):** Core xin will not change on its own—the instability of the positive feedback loop produces residuals, not directional change. No reform path exists. The garment inventory is approaching zero. The power mechanism is dormant but ever-present. The reproduction mechanism is fully operative.

## 3.3 Structural Corollaries of the Xin-Model

The following corollaries do not predict time; they predict only direction and constraints. Each can be verified or overturned by future facts.

**Corollary 1: No reform path exists.** Because core xin demands a sole center, and political reform means constraining the center or creating independent nodes. The two are structurally contradictory—the epistemic barrier shaped by core xin maximizes resistance to these signal directions. Every historical attempt at political reform within the framework of core xin (the Hundred Days' Reform, the Republic, 1989) failed, and the mode of failure was identical: epistemic barrier activation.

**Corollary 2: The garment inventory is irreversibly approaching zero.** Because historically available garments have failed one by one, and the causes of failure are irreversible changes in the external signal environment—the external source of positive feedback loop instability is continuously at work. Therefore, no new garment capable of durably wrapping core xin will appear in the future.

**Corollary 3: Economic performance as a legitimacy substitute has structural fragility.** Because legitimacy currently rests almost solely on the economy, a historically unprecedented state. Therefore, once the economy suffers prolonged deceleration, legitimacy will lack a backup support.

**Corollary 4: A republic or federation cannot operate stably as long as core xin survives.** Because even if the current container collapses, as long as core xin and the reproduction mechanism survive, the functional convergence of the positive feedback loop drives all participants' outputs to converge toward the same structure. Therefore, the rebuilt structure will, with high probability, still be a single center.

**Corollary 5: The death of core xin requires a replacement xin, not merely a shock.** Because external pressure can shatter signal containers, but external pressure alone cannot kill the positive feedback loop of core xin—a replacement loop is needed.

**Corollary 6: The cost of digital closure will continuously rise and is irreversible.** Because the share of resources required to maintain closure will continuously expand—this is the specific manifestation of the external source of positive feedback loop instability in the digital age. Therefore, closure constitutes an accelerating factor in container aging.

The six corollaries above are logical deductions from the xin-model, not wishes or warnings. If any one of them is overturned by facts, the xin-model requires a corresponding structural revision.

# Conclusion

The Accumulation Model is not a prophecy. It is a dynamical description extracted from the regression testing of ten rupture points.

What it says: the historical rhythm of Chinese civilization has a describable structure—accumulation → sawing-through → rupture → warring → hero → reconstruction → accumulation. This structure is driven by the three components of the xin-model (core xin, power mechanism, reproduction mechanism) and their ordering relationships. Its theoretical basis is the instability of positive feedback loops—positive feedback loops have no permanently sustainable equilibrium, and the Accumulation Model is the specific unfolding of this instability under the particular conditions of Chinese civilization.

What it does not say: when the next rupture will occur, where it will rupture, or what will follow.

**Contributions of this paper.** First, it provides a vertical ontological framework—xin is the sole ontological layer; institutions and economy are expressive outcomes produced by xin through positive feedback loops—offering natural explanations for the two questions that existing frameworks require additional assumptions to answer: "why active decoupling" and "why isomorphic reconstruction." Second, it demotes the Confucian-Legalist system, the Mandate of Heaven narrative, moral governance, and other elements to "garments"—concrete implementation forms of core xin—and provides an operable distinguishing tool through the Core Xin Criterion (identity composition + death criterion). Third, it derives the Accumulation Model from regression testing, describing a complete dynamical picture that existing frameworks have not integrated, anchored in the instability of positive feedback loops. Fourth, it lists five falsifiability conditions, making the xin-model a model that can be killed, not an irrefutable narrative.

A map, not navigation.

The value of the framework lies in its generality. The same three-step structure—pattern scanning, xin-model construction, regression testing—can be applied to any civilization over the long duration. Cross-civilizational extended applications and systematic comparison are the next step.
