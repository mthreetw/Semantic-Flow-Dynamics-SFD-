**Terminology**

This comparison observes each religion's attitude toward knowledge itself from the perspective of real-world knowledge-level changes and classifications, rather than the results of how each religion views knowledge. Death settlement: settlement upon death, applicable to individuals, groups, and civilizations. All proper nouns (spiritual realm, unseen, dependent origination) are labels, not theological definitions. Borrowing terms from each religion to describe epistemological limits does not imply actual scriptural meaning.

**Zhi (知, information):** In this paper, "zhi" is information—signals entering consciousness. It is not knowledge, not cognition, not understanding. "It will rain tomorrow" is a piece of zhi, regardless of whether you comprehend it, believe it, or consider it important—those are all products of zhi entering xin. Zhi is raw material; xin determines what the raw material becomes. This paper's "zhi" derives from information theory's concept of signal—signals can be copied, transmitted, and stored independently of the receiver's state. Shannon's information theory ends here. This framework picks up where Shannon leaves off: signals enter consciousness, encounter xin, and produce semantics. Shannon governs how signals travel; this framework governs what happens after they arrive.

**Xin (信):** In this paper, "xin" is not faith, not belief, not trust. Xin is the functional state of consciousness—a mixture of values, preferences, and will; one might also say the totality of human consciousness's functions: rationality plus humanity. It is present while alive, absent only in death. After the Buddha attained enlightenment, he still had preferences (choosing to teach rather than remain silent), will (forty-five years of teaching), and judgment (using different methods for different dispositions). Buddhism's "letting go" means letting go of attachment on the semantics side—specific semantics produced after zhi is processed by xin—not shutting down xin itself. Living consciousness cannot be without xin.

"Xin precedes zhi" does not mean "faith precedes knowledge." It means: before consciousness receives any information, it already has a pre-existing state for processing information (values and will). This state determines what semantics the same piece of information is processed into. This is a description of the precondition for information processing, not an epistemological thesis.


# **Introduction**
All three major religions acknowledge the existence of a domain that human reason cannot reach on its own, and each has constructed a complete system around this acknowledgment. This is not the special claim of any one religion but a gate set at the same position by three independently developed civilizational systems. The existence of the gate is an observable fact—Christianity, Islam, and Buddhism all presuppose a cognitive boundary; the difference lies in their strategies for handling it. This paper does not argue whether the boundary is epistemologically valid; it only records that all three religions behave as though it is, then analyzes their respective information flow paths on both sides of the gate.

Religion is the longest-lasting, most widely-covering meaning-generation system in human history. The three religions' strategies for handling the cognitive boundary differ—Christianity acknowledges that election and rejection have no cause, Islam acknowledges that guidance and misguidance have no cause, Buddhism acknowledges that in ultimate truth one does not know. Each has developed different strategies to handle this boundary, and on this basis constructed complete civilizational structures. Understanding how these strategies operate is an important task for understanding human civilization.

Religion is not a belief system but a system of information and meaning flow. This redefinition is not rhetorical strategy but analytical necessity. When we discuss religion as a belief system, the discussion inevitably slides into doctrinal comparison; doctrinal comparison inevitably slides into disputes of position; disputes of position have no exit. For millennia, theologians, philosophers, and atheists have walked this road countless times without ever reaching consensus. This paper chooses a different road.

If religion is an information flow system, then the tool for analyzing it should not be theology but the dynamics of information flow. This paper uses Semantic Flow Dynamics (v5.0, 40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139), a framework describing what happens when information flows between consciousnesses. The framework starts from two primitives—individual (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Individual) and stimulus (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Stimulus)—and three definitions—xin (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Xin), semantic flow (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.SemanticFlow), and signal (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Signal)—and establishes four postulates. The core postulate, the Law of Black-box (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.BlackBoxLaw), asserts that the next-moment direction of semantic flow cannot be determined by its current direction and incoming signals. From the postulates, three basic functions are derived—filtering (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Filtering), transformation (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Transformation), and collapse (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Collapse)—along with their properties, and from the basic functions, positive feedback loops (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.PositiveFeedbackLoop) are derived.

The developmental sequence between this paper and the framework must be disclosed here. The Three Religions, Five Cuts came first—the observation of structural differences among the three major religions at the cognitive level. This observation raised a question that existing theories could not answer: why can a very small number of core religious claims be amplified into entire civilizational structures, with the three religions following completely different paths? To answer this question, Semantic Flow Dynamics was constructed. This paper is the result of using the framework to go back and organize the original observation. This means the religion case is a generative case for Semantic Flow Dynamics, not a verification case—just as Newton induced universal gravitation from the apple, you cannot use the apple case to verify universal gravitation. Verification of the framework must come from independent phenomena that were not used during its construction (see Chapter Four).

Under this framework, religion presents an extremely distinctive informational phenomenon: the input end is minimal, the output end is enormous. Christianity's core claims can be compressed into the twelve sentences of the Apostles' Creed. Islam's core claim can be compressed into the single sentence of the Shahada. Buddhism's core claim can even be compressed into a single word: suffering. Yet these minimal inputs, after millennia of flow, have produced legal systems, ethical codes, artistic traditions, political institutions, wars and peace—entire civilizational structures.

This disproportionate information gain is the core problem this paper seeks to explain. How does amplification occur? What different dynamical paths did the three religions follow? Why have these religions endured for millennia while other systems that also claimed supernatural knowledge have long since perished?

The methodological stance of Semantic Flow Dynamics is: the framework does not judge the truth or falsity of semantic content (Semantic Flow Dynamics, IX, 9.5 Truth Boundary), nor does it judge good or bad, right or wrong (IX, 9.6 Value Boundary). This paper inherits this stance.

These are questions at different levels.


# **Chapter One: The Five-Stage Model—The Topology of Cognition**
The cognition of any conscious being can be mapped onto a fixed topological structure. This structure is not a product of religion but of cognition itself—religion is merely one of the systems operating on this structure. This chapter establishes this structure.

## **1.1 Starting Point: Xin**
The framework's starting point is not birth but xin.

Xin is values and will. Values are your pre-existing tendency to judge good from bad, right from wrong; will is your innate driving force. The operational logic of all three major religions presupposes that xin precedes zhi—they all first require an attitude adjustment before transmitting knowledge. This is not coincidence but structural necessity: without xin, beyond-boundary knowledge cannot enter; the Bible would be merely a historical document, the Quran merely an Arabic text, Buddhist sutras merely philosophical treatises. "Xin precedes zhi" is not an epistemological thesis proposed by this paper but an operational premise shared by all three religions—this paper observes this premise without arguing whether it is epistemologically correct. Values may collapse and reorganize after contact with information; that is the transformation process and does not change the temporal sequence.

Xin determines what semantics zhi becomes. The same piece of zhi, entering different xin, produces completely different semantics. The same sentence "It will rain tomorrow," entering a farmer's xin versus an office worker's xin, produces vastly different semantics. Not just different reactions—different meanings, different levels of importance, different associative extensions, different behaviors driven. Zhi is material; xin determines everything about the material.

The first article of all three religions is an attitude, not knowledge. Christianity begins with fearing the Lord. Islam begins with fearing Allah. Buddhism begins with nothing—the denial of a first cause is itself an attitudinal stance. None of the three starts by giving you a pile of information and letting you judge; all first require you to adjust the state of your xin, then begin transmitting knowledge. This is not coincidence but structural necessity: without xin as the activation condition, knowledge is noise (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Noise)—signals arrive, are processed, and nothing grows. Religion cannot stand without xin; this is not rhetoric but a dynamical fact of information flow.

## **1.2 Inside the Cognitive Boundary: Civilization's Main Battlefield**
After xin is activated, consciousness begins receiving, processing, and producing knowledge. This process occurs inside the cognitive boundary—the domain where humans can experience, reason, and verify.

The inside domain is the main battlefield of human civilization-building. Agriculture, architecture, law, medicine, science, art—all observable, verifiable, accumulable knowledge achievements reside in this domain. This domain operates without religion; science and philosophy do well here.

But the inside domain has one religion-relevant special phenomenon: a small amount of knowledge from beyond the boundary is amplified here into entire civilizational structures. This amplification process is the subject of Chapter Three. Here we need only mark the position: the inside domain is not only natural knowledge's territory but also the soil where supernatural knowledge takes root.

## **1.3 The Cognitive Boundary: The Gate**
The cognitive boundary is the core node of the five-stage model. It is the boundary where humans acknowledge "I don't know"—not because information is insufficient (that can be remedied by collection) but because the nature of the problem exceeds the capacity of human cognitive tools.

What happens after death? Why does the universe exist rather than not? What is the nature of consciousness? What is the ultimate ground of morality? All three religions place these questions in a domain that human reason cannot resolve on its own—whether or not this placement is epistemologically valid, it is an operational premise shared by all three, and it is where the gate is set.

The cognitive boundary is not a wall but a door—and the key that opens it is xin. Without xin, the boundary is a wall: one stands on this side, acknowledges "I don't know," and stops. Philosophy takes this road; Socrates' "I know that I know nothing" is a frank acceptance of the boundary. Science takes another road: attempting to push the boundary forward without crossing it—science handles only falsifiable propositions; questions beyond the boundary are outside its jurisdiction.

Religion takes a third road: directly claiming to have transported knowledge from the other side of the door. Revelation, enlightenment, divine mandate—all are claims that "knowledge from beyond the boundary has arrived inside." And the believer's xin determines whether they accept this transport. If xin does not open, the gate does not open, and knowledge cannot enter.

## **1.4 Beyond the Cognitive Boundary: The Claimed Source of Religious Knowledge**
The outside domain is the unverifiable knowledge domain. "Unverifiable" does not equal "nonexistent," nor does it equal "existent"—it means that humanity's existing cognitive tools cannot render judgment on the contents of this domain.

The three religions describe this domain completely differently. Christianity calls it the spiritual realm, claiming it is real, active, conscious, capable of revelation and deception. Islam calls it the unseen (al-ghayb), claiming it is the foundation of all created order, capable of guidance and misguidance. Buddhism's approach is fundamentally different—it does not name this domain, does not describe it, and even questions whether it needs to be discussed. "In ultimate truth: unknown" is Buddhism's most honest attitude toward the beyond.

This paper uses the neutral term "beyond the cognitive boundary" without employing any religion's proper nouns. Spiritual realm, unseen, ultimate truth—these are labels borrowed by each religion, not analytical categories of this paper. This paper needs only one fact: all three religions acknowledge the existence of a domain that human reason cannot reach; the disagreement is about this domain's nature and its relationship to humans.

## **1.5 Death: The Antithesis of Xin**
Death is the terminal of information flow. In Semantic Flow Dynamics terminology: consciousness disappears, xin and semantics simultaneously reset to zero, with no process and no stages (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.DeathLaw, P4 the Law of Death). What can be left behind are only signal containers (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.SignalContainer)—books, institutions, architecture, images. But signals are not semantics; reading a dead person's book produces semantics that belong to the reader, not the deceased. The deceased's semantics no longer exist anywhere.

Death is not only a terminal but the antithesis of xin. Xin activates all semantics; death terminates all semantics. This opposition constitutes the tension of the entire system: if death is the absolute end, then all semantics activated by xin ultimately reset to zero—what is the point? This question was not invented by philosophers; it is a structural predicament that every consciousness will eventually collide with.

Death settlement. Death is the settlement point, but the one who settles is not the deceased—it is the living. You die; others judge your life. A religion dies; history judges that religion. A civilization dies; later civilizations judge it. You have no voice from inside the coffin. Death settlement is this paper's analytical perspective, not a negation of religious eschatology—this paper does not judge whether reincarnation or eternal life is true; it only marks an observable fact: from external observation, death is the settlement point. Buddhists believe there is a next life, but from the perspective of death settlement, this life's settlement has already been completed in others' eyes. What you believe does not affect how others judge you. Death settlement applies equally to individuals, groups, religions, and civilizations—once the coffin lid is closed, it is closed.

Death is the hardest event beyond the cognitive boundary. No scientific method can tell you what happens after death—not "currently cannot" but "in principle cannot," because death is unrepeatable, unobservable, and unverifiable. It forces the entire system from the terminal end to provide an answer. Xin activates the system; death compels the system to be complete. The tension between the two ends is the dynamical reason for religion's existence.

## **1.6 The Structural Foundation of the Five Stages**
The five stages are not an arbitrary division but a structural fact of cognition. Three of them are physical conditions: the inside domain is the verifiable knowledge domain, the cognitive boundary is the limit of human reason, and death is the physical termination of consciousness. These three do not depend on any religious doctrine; they hold even if all religions are removed. The starting point (xin) is a stance adopted by the framework—this framework maintains that any consciousness already possesses values and will before receiving information (see the discussion in 1.1); this stance is debatable, but the framework departs from it. The only "soft" element is the outside domain—its existence is a fact (human reason has a boundary; beyond the boundary there logically exists a domain), but what is in this domain is claimed.

These three lines (xin, boundary, death) cut cognitive space into exactly two domains (inside, outside). Remove xin and the system has no activation condition; remove death and the system has no terminal; remove the boundary and the verifiable merges with the unverifiable, erasing the distinction between religion and science. No more parsimonious partition has been found—any additional dividing line can be subsumed into the internal structure of the inside or outside domain without requiring a new topological position. If someone can propose an alternative topology requiring fewer or more nodes, with explanatory power equal to or exceeding this model, this paper's structure needs revision.


# **Chapter Two: Definition and Position of Beyond-Boundary Knowledge**
This chapter does not argue for the source of beyond-boundary knowledge. The source question is outside this paper's jurisdiction—just as Semantic Flow Dynamics does not explain where xin comes from (xin is a primitive, irreducible), this paper does not explain where beyond-boundary knowledge comes from. What this chapter does is more limited and more solid: define what beyond-boundary knowledge is, locate its position in the five-stage model, and record each religion's source claim.

## **2.1 Definition**
The operational definition of beyond-boundary knowledge: knowledge that religious systems claim comes from the other side of the cognitive boundary.

This definition has only one criterion: the claim. This paper does not judge whether the claim is true—is "there is judgment after death" truly from divine revelation, or is it a human construction? This paper does not handle this question. It records only a fact: Christianity claims this knowledge comes from God, Islam claims it comes from Allah, Buddhism claims it comes from the direct realization of the awakened. The claim itself is an observable fact; no judgment of its truth is required.

This definition inherits the channel (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Channel) definition from Semantic Flow Dynamics: channels carry self-declared signals but do not carry verifiable provenance. This paper records the existence and behavior of claims without adjudicating their correctness.

## **2.2 Why the Source Is Not Argued**
Readers may expect this paper to argue that beyond-boundary knowledge "truly" comes from the supernatural, or to argue that it is "merely" a human construction. This paper does neither, not from evasion but from methodological consistency.

Xin is a primitive of Semantic Flow Dynamics—the framework departs from it without being responsible for explaining it. The source of xin is a black box (irreducible, inexhaustible, observable only through its effects). Beyond-boundary knowledge occupies the same epistemological position in this paper: it is a premise of analysis, not a conclusion. Each of the three religions claims to possess knowledge from beyond the boundary; this claim, after entering the inside domain, produces observable effects—amplification into civilizational structures. This paper analyzes the effects, not the source.

This approach has an important consequence: it allows this paper to be read by both believers and non-believers. Believers may hold that beyond-boundary knowledge truly comes from God; non-believers may hold that it is a human construction—neither affects this paper's analysis, because the paper analyzes the flow path of knowledge after it enters the inside domain, not its starting point beyond the boundary. What the source is does not change how the amplification mechanism operates, just as where a seed comes from does not change the process by which it grows into a forest.

## **2.3 The Source Claims of the Three Religions**
The three religions' source claims for beyond-boundary knowledge are completely different, and these differences directly affect subsequent information flow paths. Here we record the claims without judgment.

Christianity claims: beyond-boundary knowledge comes from divine revelation. God is an active, conscious, purposeful being who chooses to reveal to humanity. The channels of revelation are prophets and apostles; the record of revelation is the Bible. But Christianity simultaneously acknowledges an unsettling fact: beyond the boundary there is not only revelation but also deception. The spiritual realm is active, capable of both revelation and deception, and human reason cannot distinguish between the two. This means the believer's xin is not built on rational judgment—reason cannot help here—but on xin itself. Election and rejection have no cause; revelation may increase.

Islam claims: beyond-boundary knowledge comes from divine revelation (wahy) from Allah. Similar to Christianity, the unseen is active and conscious, capable of guidance and misguidance. But there is one key difference from Christianity: Islam directly denies the increase of revelation. Muhammad is the Seal of the Prophets, the Quran is the final revelation, and the channel has been permanently closed. This means the stock of beyond-boundary knowledge is fixed—there will be no new input. Guidance and misguidance have no cause; human reason (ʿaql) likewise cannot distinguish between them.

Buddhism's claim is fundamentally different. Buddhism does not claim that an external source is actively transmitting knowledge. What Buddhism claims is: the awakened, through practice, crossed the boundary and attained direct cognition on the other side. The Buddha is not a channel—he is not "an intermediary transporting knowledge from beyond the boundary for you"—but a pioneer, "the first person to arrive there, telling you how to arrive yourself." Furthermore, Buddhism is not even certain "what is there" beyond the boundary—in ultimate truth: unknown. The distinction between conventional and ultimate truth cannot be crossed by speculation. Buddhism's attitude toward the beyond comes closest to frank unknowing.

## **2.4 The Dynamical Effects of Source Claims**
Although this paper does not adjudicate the truth of source claims, source claims themselves, as signals entering the inside domain, produce different dynamical effects. These effects are observable and fall within this paper's analytical scope.

Christianity's "revelation may increase" means the channel is open—new beyond-boundary knowledge may arrive at any time. This creates a dynamic signal environment: believers must continually discern what is new revelation and what is deception. The church as an institutionalized discernment mechanism thus becomes necessary.

Islam's "revelation will not increase" means the channel is closed—the stock of beyond-boundary knowledge is fixed in the Quran and Hadith. This creates a closed signal environment: all answers are already in existing texts; what is needed is interpretation, not new input. This explains the high development of Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh)—when input is fixed, the refinement of interpretation becomes the sole direction of systemic evolution.

Buddhism's "individually accessible" means the channel is not designated but open to all practitioners. This creates a dispersed signal environment: every awakened being is a potential channel, with no central institution monopolizing the transmission of beyond-boundary knowledge. This explains the pluralism of Buddhist traditions—when the channel is not monopolized, different practitioners bring back different "reports" from their own practice; pluralism is a structural inevitability.

These three differences in source claims—open channel, closed channel, no central channel—directly determine the dynamical path of each of the three religions. The next chapter will analyze these three paths in detail.


# **Chapter Three: Dynamical Paths—Three Different Modes of Civilizational Generation**
This chapter is the core of the paper. The problem to be explained is one of information gain: the input end is minimal (a few core claims), the output end is enormous (entire civilizational structures). From the Apostles' Creed's twelve sentences to the entire social order of medieval Europe, from the Shahada's single sentence to the Islamic civilization spanning three continents, from the Four Noble Truths to the Buddhist cultural sphere across East and Southeast Asia—how does amplification occur?

The three religions share the basic elements of amplification—signal transformation, functional convergence, institutionalization of signal containers, and death pressure—but the way these elements combine is completely different, producing three utterly distinct dynamical paths. This is not a single template applied three times, but three modes of civilizational generation each with its own logic.

## **3.1 Shared Amplification Elements**
Before expanding the three paths, we first locate the amplification elements shared by all religions.

**Signal transformation.** Beyond-boundary knowledge enters the inside domain, is transformed within believers' semantic flow, and produces new semantics. The four words "God loves the world" enter a believer's xin, undergo transformation, and can produce an entire ethics of love's nature, the meaning of sacrifice, and the conditions of forgiveness. The single word "suffering" enters a practitioner's xin and can unfold into the complete system of the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path. The product of transformation is new—it is neither a copy of zhi nor a projection of xin (transformation: the process by which a signal is processed within semantic flow, whose outcome cannot be determined by the input). The same core claim unfolds into completely different results in different cultures—because the signal is the same but xin differs, and the products of transformation are various.

**Functional convergence.** Individual semantics functionally converge at the group level. Functional convergence (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.FunctionalConvergence) is not semantic convergence—the Law of Black-box guarantees that every individual's semantic flow is different—but the statistical approximation of output signal patterns. Large numbers of consciousnesses each perform transformation on the core claim, and the output-end signal patterns happen to align—similar vocabulary, similar behavioral norms, similar emotional responses. From external observation, this is what "church," "ummah," and "sangha" are. Functional convergence pushes individual transformation products from the private domain into the public domain, forming positive feedback loops.

**Institutionalization of signal containers.** The products of functional convergence are encoded into signal containers—canons, rituals, ecclesiastical hierarchies, legal systems, architecture. Signal containers do not think and do not produce semantic flow, but they delay drift and span generations. Canons can persist for millennia; institutions can persist for centuries. Each new generation of consciousnesses receives these signal containers and re-transforms them in their own semantic flow; the stability of the core signal ensures that output patterns are statistically close to the previous generation's. The continuity of civilization is not the transmission of semantics but the statistical reproduction of output patterns.

**Death pressure.** Death as a continuous pressure source makes consciousness repeatedly return to the system seeking meaning. Every consciousness eventually faces death; each encounter reactivates the positive feedback loop. Death pressure is a driving force shared by all religions, and also a key distinction between religion and most secular ideologies—secular ideologies typically respond to death with lower intensity; their positive feedback loops depend more on the fulfillment of this-worldly conditions, and once reality signals contradict the claims, the loop easily breaks.

These four elements are necessary conditions. But the key is not in the elements themselves but in how they combine. The three religions used the same elements to follow completely different paths.

## **3.2 Christianity's Path: Evolution and the Gu-King**
Christianity's dynamical path is: transformation → functional convergence → formation of positive feedback loop → drift → schism → collision → mutual slaughter → survivor evolution → further schism → further slaughter → the Gu-King emerges victorious.

The dynamical characteristic of this path is: open channel plus unlocked signal equals variation constantly produced. Christianity claims that revelation may increase (Three Religions, Five Cuts, Row IV); the channel is open; new beyond-boundary knowledge may arrive at any time. This means the signal end continuously receives new input; believers must discern what is new revelation and what is deception; the church as an institutionalized discernment mechanism thus becomes necessary. But the standard of discernment itself is not locked—different standards produce different churches, different churches produce different doctrinal interpretations, and schism is a structural inevitability.

After schism comes collision. Every major schism in Christian history—the East-West Schism, the Reformation, the Counter-Reformation—was accompanied by intense competition and even violence. Collision produces selection pressure: branches with weak adaptability shrink or perish; branches with strong adaptability survive and evolve. Catholicism, Orthodoxy, and Protestantism are survivors of several rounds of elimination. Each survivor is more refined, more organized, and more resistant to the next challenge than before the schism.

This is not amplification but evolution. Amplification is merely growth in volume; evolution is structural variation plus selection pressure plus increased adaptability. Christianity's dynamics are not simply "core claims amplified into civilization"—core claims enter positive feedback loops and are continually reinterpreted, reorganized, and re-adapted through repeated schism and collision; the versions that survive are still recognizable on the signal end compared to the originals, but on the institutional and practical ends they are barely recognizable.

The competition structure: market competition plus heresy judgment. Market competition means competing for believers and influence. Heresy judgment is a zero-sum game: your doctrine is wrong; you must be destroyed. The Inquisition, heresy trials, and excommunication are all institutionalizations of the zero-sum game.

The evolutionary path's prediction: Christianity will continue to split; after splitting, the branches will compete; the survivors of competition will be stronger. This prediction can be examined against historical data—if Christianity's splitting trend stops, or if post-split branches do not compete, the evolutionary path's descriptive power is weakened.

## **3.3 Islam's Path: Lockdown and Steady State**
Islam's dynamical path is: transformation → functional convergence → formation of positive feedback loop → signal lockdown → variation suppressed → further suppression → stable community.

The dynamical characteristic of this path is: closed channel plus extreme signal lockdown equals variation suppressed. Islam directly denies the increase of revelation (Three Religions, Five Cuts, Row IV); Muhammad is the Seal of the Prophets; the Quran is the final revelation; the channel has been permanently closed. The stock of beyond-boundary knowledge is fixed; there will be no new input.

After channel closure, the system's only direction of evolution is interpretation. The principle that the Quran cannot be translated requires believers to access the Arabic original directly, locking the signal at its most original form. The Hadith chain of transmission (isnad) system traces the complete transmission path of each hadith, recording every intermediate node—in Semantic Flow Dynamics terminology, a complete record of the channel. This is one of the most systematic signal-provenance tracking systems in human history. When input is fixed, the refinement of interpretation becomes the sole direction of systemic evolution, explaining the high development of Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh).

The lockdown strategy has its costs. The signal end is highly unified, but schism exists nonetheless—the Sunni-Shia split is not on the signal end (they read the same Quran) but in the contest over interpretive authority (different judgments on succession and authority). From the Law of Black-box: signal lockdown does not equal semantic lockdown. Unified signals plus different xin produce interpretive divergence. But unlike Christianity's evolutionary path, Islam's schism does not produce continuous evolutionary pressure, because both sides of the split acknowledge the same locked signal end. The contest is over who has the authority to interpret, not whether the signal itself should change.

The lockdown path's prediction: Islam's signal end will maintain high stability; variation will concentrate on the interpretive and political ends; there will be no fundamental rewriting of the signal end. This prediction can be examined against historical data and contemporary observation.

## **3.4 Buddhism's Path: Self-Organization and Natural Selection**
Buddhism's dynamical path is: transformation → individual practice → emergence and extinction.

Buddhism faces the same cognitive map (the five-stage structure) but operates at a fundamentally different level. Christianity and Islam operate on the signal end—revelation descends, is written into canons, and is locked or screened. Buddhism operates on the semantics side—it points toward what is inside the black box: unsayable, untransmittable, unverifiable. The Buddha is not a channel but a pioneer—"the first person to arrive there, telling you how to arrive yourself." Sutras are not knowledge but signals pointing toward semantics—the finger pointing at the moon; the finger is not the moon.

The dynamical characteristic of this path is: no central channel, no signal lockdown, only market competition, no zero-sum game. Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana are not Gu-Kings that split off from each other but grew independently. "Grew independently" does not mean no competition—the number of sanghas a region can support is limited; alms resources are scarce; the debate traditions between schools (as at Nalanda) are institutionalized signal-end competition. But the competition here has only a market mechanism: competing for followers, alms, and influence; schools with weak adaptability shrink; those with strong adaptability expand.

Buddhism's competition structure has a fundamental difference from the Abrahamic religions: no heresy. Christianity's evolutionary path has two layers of competition—market mechanism plus zero-sum game. The market mechanism competes for believers; the zero-sum game is heresy judgment: your doctrine is wrong; you must be destroyed. The Inquisition, heresy trials, excommunication—all are institutionalizations of the zero-sum game. Islam has the same two layers; the difference is that the zero-sum game's criterion is interpretive authority rather than doctrine. Buddhism has only the first layer. Skillful means (upāya) fundamentally eliminates the possibility of zero-sum games—you take your path, I take mine; both are fingers pointing at the moon; different fingers do not constitute grounds for mutual destruction.

Why does Buddhism not produce a heresy institution? Because awakening is an individual event, not an institutional certification. Whether you have awakened, only you know; no institution has the authority to stamp its approval. Heresy judgment in Christianity and Islam can operate because there is a universally acknowledged stamping institution—the church, the ummah. The stamping institution's authority derives from the channel: we have a connection to the beyond, so we have the authority to judge whether you are right. Buddhism lacks this structure. Individual schools occasionally exhibit exclusionary behavior (Nichiren declaring other schools heretical, Gelug suppressing Jonang), but these behaviors cannot be institutionalized into a persistent heresy judgment mechanism—because the adjudicator's baseline is not acknowledged by other schools. Without a stamping institution, there is no heresy institution.

The information flow modes within Buddhism are not uniform. From the perspective of signal flow direction, at least three different modes exist. The first is the ascending mode, represented by Chan/Zen and Theravada vipassana: individuals through practice touch the boundary themselves, with no external input, no designated channel—the purest self-organization. The second is the quasi-descending mode, represented by Pure Land Buddhism: Amitabha's forty-eight vows are an external promise; believers rely on faith-aspiration-practice for rebirth; the operational structure approaches revelation descending, and the signal end even has lockdown characteristics—nianfo (reciting the Buddha's name) is repeatedly receiving the same signal. But the key difference is: this "promise" does not claim to come from active transmission from beyond the boundary, but is a path option provided within the framework. The third is the channel-designation mode, represented by Tibetan Buddhism: tulku reincarnation, empowerment lineage, the guru as a necessary condition for practice—the channel is institutionally designated. But what is designated differs from Christianity's ecclesiastical hierarchy—what is designated is "who is qualified to guide your practice," not "who has the authority to transmit revelation."

These three modes have coexisted within Buddhism for over a millennium, with none eliminating the other two. This itself is a structural product of pure market competition (no zero-sum game)—without heresy judgment to force uniformity, without a stamping institution to eliminate deviators, multi-mode coexistence is a steady state, not a transitional state. This coexistence would not occur within the Abrahamic religions, because the zero-sum game layer would squeeze out modes that deviate from the main path. Buddhism's internal diversity is not a sign of low framework descriptive precision but a structural characteristic of the object itself—an X-ray of a crab looks blurrier than an X-ray of a human, but that is because the crab has no endoskeleton, not because the X-ray is faulty.

The costs of the self-organization path must be recorded simultaneously. Pure market competition, no doctrinal baseline, no heresy judgment—these characteristics, while producing pluralism and resilience, also produce structural fragility. First, organizational looseness: the absence of central authority means sangha corruption cannot be systematically corrected; disciplinary laxity has no institutional response mechanism. Second, lack of resistance: the extinction of Buddhism in its Indian homeland was not merely an external event of "signal containers being destroyed"—facing military invasion by a highly organized lockdown-path religion like Islam, the self-organization path lacked even the institutional capacity to mobilize resistance. This is an endogenous defect of the path, not merely historical misfortune.

Here is an apparently contradictory phenomenon: Buddhism claims the unsayable, yet the volume of Buddhist texts exceeds that of Christianity and Islam combined. The root of the contradiction is precisely a structural product of semantics-side operation. Semantics is unsayable, but to convey "semantics is unsayable" you must use signals—using language to explain why language cannot reach there, using concepts to argue why concepts are inadequate. Every sutra is a finger pointing at the moon, and skillful means allows fingers to proliferate infinitely—each disposition requires a different finger, and different fingers are different sutras. Christianity's signals are screened through evolution: few survive but those that do are strong. Islam's signals are compressed through lockdown: few and hard. Buddhism's signals proliferate through skillful means: many and soft.

Buddhism is not the area where this framework's explanatory power is weakest; quite the contrary—Buddhism is the most extreme case of the postulates. The Law of Black-box says the next-moment direction of semantic flow cannot be determined by its current direction and incoming signals; the Law of Dissipation (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.DissipationLaw) says semantic flow cannot be reduced to signals. Buddhists read sutras, meditate, observe precepts; signals enter in large quantities, the postulates take effect in large quantities, semantics are produced in large quantities. "Unsayable" does not mean no semantics; it means semantics too abundant for language to contain. Buddhism's canon is larger than Christianity's and Islam's combined, but its degree of institutionalization is the lowest—not because semantics is scarce, but because the outlet for semantics is different. Christianity's and Islam's semantics flow back to the signal end—becoming doctrine, law, and institutions. Buddhism's semantics remain on the consciousness end—becoming behavior, habit, lifestyle, and cultural temperament. Low externalization does not equal low output.

The self-organization path's prediction: competition among Buddhist traditions operates on a pure market mechanism, producing neither heresy judgment nor doctrinal war, but the rate of school rise-and-fall is no lower than the other two religions. The core claim (the Four Noble Truths) maintains convergence across all traditions, but institutional forms are highly diverse, and multiple information flow modes coexist over long periods. Historically sporadic exclusionary behaviors (Nichiren, Gelug) depend on external political conditions and cannot be sustainably institutionalized. These predictions can be examined against observation.

## **3.5 Comparison of the Three Paths**
The differences among the three paths are not in the elements of amplification—transformation, functional convergence, institutionalization, and death pressure are present in every religion—but in how the elements combine and what dynamical behaviors they produce. Christianity's open channel plus no lockdown equals signal-end evolution. Islam's closed channel plus extreme lockdown equals steady state. Buddhism's no central channel plus no lockdown equals self-organization. All three have competition; the difference is in its structure: Christianity and Islam have market competition plus a zero-sum game (heresy judgment); Buddhism has pure market competition only (no heresy institution).

The intensity of death pressure differs across the three paths. Christianity's and Islam's death pressure is ultimate—there is only one chance; this life is the sole testing ground; the driving force is concentrated and urgent. Buddhism's death pressure is iterative—there is a next life; pressure is distributed across multiple rebirths; the driving force is dispersed and long-term. This explains why the evolutionary and lockdown paths typically produce faster civilizational expansion than the self-organization path.

The three paths have yet another, deeper dimension of difference: the return direction of semantics. Christianity's and Islam's semantics flow back to the signal end—doctrine, law, and institutions. After transformation, the products are re-encoded into signal containers: creeds, canon law, Islamic jurisprudence. This is the externalization path—semantics return from consciousness to signal, from the individual to institutions. The externalization path is easy to observe, easy to quantify, easy to institutionalize, so civilizational expansion is fast, but also fragile—destroy the signal containers and the institutions collapse. Buddhism's semantics do not flow back to the signal end but remain on the consciousness end—becoming behavior, habit, lifestyle, cultural temperament. Tea ceremony, calligraphy, mindfulness, attitudes toward death—all are products of semantics bypassing signal containers to directly change how people live. This is the internalization path—semantics flow from consciousness to behavior, from knowledge to life. The internalization path is hard to observe, hard to quantify, hard to institutionalize, so civilizational expansion is slow, but resilience is extremely strong—you can demolish temples, but you cannot strip already-internalized semantics from a people's daily behavior. This explains Buddhism's cultural saturation phenomenon in East and Southeast Asia: "to be Thai is to be Buddhist" not because Thailand has a powerful Buddhist institution, but because Buddhist semantics have been internalized into the Thai way of life.

An important observation: signal unity does not equal semantic unity, and signal plurality does not equal semantic divergence. Islam is most unified on the signal end, yet the Sunni-Shia split still occurred. Buddhism is most plural on the signal end (canonical versions, languages, and lineages all differ), yet the Four Noble Truths are acknowledged across all traditions. The root of drift lies in differences in xin—the same signal processed by different xin produces different semantics; semantics are then externalized as different signal outputs (doctrine, institutions, practice), and divergence becomes observable at this point. This supports the predictions of the Semantic Flow Dynamics postulates.

The direction of knowledge flow is another dimension of path difference. Christianity's and Islam's knowledge flow is revelation descending—from beyond the cognitive boundary to inside it; direction is unidirectional; the channel is designated. Christianity's channel chain is: God → prophets → apostles → church → believers. Islam's channel chain is shorter: Allah → Muhammad → Quran → ummah. Buddhism's knowledge flow is introspection ascending—the direction is completely reversed; individuals through practice touch the boundary themselves; the channel is not designated but individually accessible. In the descending mode, xin's gravitational pull concentrates on the channel holder, readily producing centralized religious institutions; in the ascending mode, xin's gravitational pull is naturally dispersed, and the degree of institutionalization is comparatively lower.

The three religions' strategies for responding to death are completely different but functionally equivalent—all are semantic encodings of "consciousness terminates, semantics reset to zero." Christianity encodes death as a passage (the door to eternal life)—hope strengthens xin's intensity. Islam encodes death as judgment (the beginning of the final reckoning)—death pressure transforms into constraining power over present behavior. Buddhism encodes death as transformation (one link in the cycle of rebirth)—death pressure transforms into motivation for practice. The shared effect of all three encodings: re-encoding death from "the end of semantics" into "the continuation condition of semantics"—death is no longer the system's terminator but part of the system.

## **3.6 The Civilization Arena: Path Is Not a Sufficient Condition**
The three paths explain how the three religions walked their roads, but not why some religions that walked similar roads died. Manichaeism had a claim to beyond-boundary knowledge, had transformation, had functional convergence, had institutionalization, had death pressure—all elements were present. Yet Manichaeism died. Zoroastrianism too.

Path is a necessary condition, not a sufficient condition. History is an arena. Path qualifies you to enter the arena; whether you survive depends on conditions in the arena—political protection or persecution, geographic diffusion or confinement, symbiosis or conflict with power structures. But paths themselves also have endogenous fragility. Buddhism's extinction in its Indian homeland cannot be entirely attributed to external conditions—the self-organization path's lack of institutional capacity for mobilizing resistance is a structural defect of the path, not merely historical misfortune. Similarly, Manichaeism had all the elements, but was simultaneously persecuted by the Roman Empire and the Sasanian Empire; its signal containers were systematically destroyed; the consciousnesses carrying xin were systematically eliminated. Generational rupture was not naturally occurring but was forcibly executed. The complete process tracing of Manichaeism—which element failed at which stage due to which specific historical conditions—is a direction for subsequent research; here we only mark the position.

Death settlement applies equally to all civilizations. Manichaeism's coffin is closed; history has already judged it. The three major religions' coffins are not yet closed; they are still in the arena. This paper analyzes the three that are still in the arena—not because they are more worthy of analysis, but because they are still alive and their signal-end behavior is still observable.


# **Chapter Four: The Framework's Limitations and Challenge Conditions**

## **4.1 Problems the Framework Does Not Handle**
This paper does not handle the truth of religion, its social function, psychological function, or political function. It handles only how information flows, what paths it takes, and what strategies resist drift. The judgments this paper makes are causal mechanism judgments (e.g., channel closure causes interpretation to become the sole direction of evolution); these judgments are a necessary component of analysis.

The judgments this paper makes are: structural judgments at the epistemological level (e.g., human reason has a boundary) and causal mechanism judgments (e.g., channel closure causes interpretation to become the sole direction of evolution). These judgments are necessary components of analysis and do not contradict "not judging doctrinal truth."

## **4.2 Structural Limitation: The Semantics Side Cannot Be Quantified**
This paper's core mechanisms—signal transformation and semantic transformation—occur on the semantics side. The semantics side cannot be quantified; this is not a defect of this framework but a structural ceiling of human cognition. To explain what happens internally, you must use language; language is itself an output of semantics; using output to explain the process that produces output is self-referential and necessarily collapses. This limitation applies equally to all human theories, not just this framework. Any theory claiming to fully quantify "what happens in the human mind" is either quantifying proxy indicators on the signal side, or collapsing in self-reference.

This paper chooses to face this ceiling honestly. What can be quantified (the signal side) is honestly quantified; what cannot be quantified (the semantics side) is honestly acknowledged as such. Indirect verification paths exist—if the transformation mechanism holds, the institutional outputs of the same core claim across different cultures should display predictable variation patterns, and this can be tested. But directly quantifying semantics itself is in principle impossible.

## **4.3 Challenge Conditions**
**Challenge One: Alternative explanations for the paths.** If someone can use a theory not involving signal transformation—such as pure institutional inertia, power structures, or economic interests—to fully explain the differences among the three dynamical paths, with explanatory power equal to or exceeding this paper's, then Chapter Three's argument is weakened.

**Challenge Two: Failure of path predictions.** This paper designates testable predictions for each of the three paths: Christianity will continue to split and post-split branches will compete; Islam's signal end will remain stable with variation concentrated on the interpretive end; Buddhism's competition is pure market mechanism (no heresy judgment) with a school elimination rate no lower than the other two. If these predictions are systematically refuted—e.g., Christianity's splitting trend stops, or Islam sees fundamental rewriting of the signal end—then the corresponding path description needs revision. It must be admitted that these predictions are too safe—anyone familiar with the history of religion could make similar predictions without this framework. For the framework to prove itself more than terminology packaging, it needs to make precise, differentiated predictions about phenomena that existing theories cannot predict. The most promising direction is civilizational collision—when religions of different paths meet, what happens on the signal end and the semantics side respectively? Such predictions can both demonstrate the framework's unique explanatory power and be tested against historical and contemporary cases.

**Challenge Three: Redundancy of the five-stage model.** If someone can demonstrate that one of the five stages can be absorbed into the others—e.g., the cognitive boundary can be subsumed into a continuous gradient within the inside domain—then the model's topology needs revision.

**Challenge Four: Challenge to the underlying framework.** This paper inherits all challenge conditions of Semantic Flow Dynamics. If the underlying framework is overturned, the upper-level analysis simultaneously loses validity. This paper's validity is bound to the validity of Semantic Flow Dynamics.

## **4.4 Theoretical Positioning: What Different Frameworks See from the Same Problem**
This paper's core question is: how do very few core religious claims get amplified into entire civilizational structures, with the three religions following three completely different paths? This question is not unique to this framework—existing theories have each touched it from different angles. This section does not attempt a panoramic literature review but selects three theories most directly relevant to this paper, subjecting them to stress tests against the same phenomenon: what can each explain, where does each stop, and can this framework pick up where they leave off?

### **4.4.1 Sperber: Epidemiology of Representations**
Dan Sperber's (1996) epidemiology of representations is the existing theory closest to this framework. His core claim is nearly isomorphic to this paper's postulates: cultural representations in interpersonal transmission are not copied but transformed. Every transmission involves the receiver's reconstruction; therefore cultural stability cannot be explained by copying, only by "the convergence of large numbers of transformations in statistical terms." This overlaps highly with this framework's postulate (Law of Black-box: the next-moment direction of semantic flow cannot be determined by its current direction and incoming signals) and group-level functional convergence.

**What Sperber can explain.** He can explain why religious concepts spread easily—because certain representations have "attractors" for human cognition; their structure happens to fit the default tendencies of the human brain. Supernatural agency is a strong attractor: the human brain innately tends to over-attribute agency (Barrett 2004); therefore representations like "conscious supernatural beings" are not easily discarded in transmission but are repeatedly reconstructed. Sperber's framework can explain why all major religions contain claims of supernatural agency—this is the effect of cognitive attractors.

**Where Sperber stops.** He stops at the problem of path divergence. If the spread of religious concepts is primarily driven by cognitive attractors, then all religions should follow similar paths—because human cognitive architecture is shared and attractors act similarly on all cultures. But the facts say otherwise. Christianity followed an evolutionary path, Islam followed a lockdown path, and Buddhism followed a self-organization path. The differences among these three paths cannot be explained by cognitive attractors, because all three faced the same batch of human brains.

Specific case: Islam's signal-end lockdown. The principle that the Quran cannot be translated requires Muslims worldwide to access the Arabic original; the hadith chain of transmission (isnad) system traces the complete transmission path of each hadith. Sperber's framework has no tool to describe this strategy of "deliberately suppressing transformation"—his model assumes transformation occurs naturally and focuses on convergence patterns after transformation, not on institutional designs that intervene in the transformation process. More critically, even under conditions of extreme signal-end lockdown, the Sunni-Shia split still occurred. The split is not on the signal end (both read the same Quran) but in the contest over interpretive authority driven by differences in xin. Sperber's framework does not distinguish between signal and xin and therefore cannot capture the phenomenon of "signal unity but different xin leading to interpretive divergence."

**Where this framework picks up.** Two additional tools in this framework solve the problem where Sperber stopped. First, "xin" as a primitive independent of signal. Sperber's attractors are properties of cognitive structure—they explain which representations are easily accepted, but not why equally easily accepted representations follow completely different paths in different systems. This framework's "xin" (values and will) provides the explanation for path divergence: the three religions' core claims enter different xin, transforming into different dynamical behaviors. Second, the hard boundary between the signal side and the semantics side. This boundary enables the framework to describe "signal unity but semantic divergence" and "signal plurality but semantic convergence" (as in the Four Noble Truths being acknowledged across all Buddhist traditions)—two phenomena that Sperber's framework cannot distinguish.

### **4.4.2 Luhmann: Social Systems Theory**
Niklas Luhmann's social systems theory made an architectural choice directly opposite to this framework's: he excluded consciousness from the social system. In Luhmann's system, the basic unit of social systems is not consciousness but communication. Consciousness belongs to the system's "environment," not its "interior." This is not an oversight—Luhmann explicitly knew that once consciousness is placed inside the system, the system cannot handle consciousness's unpredictability. He chose to go around it.

**What Luhmann can explain.** He can excellently explain how religion operates as a functional subsystem. The religious system handles the binary code of "transcendence/immanence"—religion transforms the indeterminate into determinate communication. Doctrine, rituals, ecclesiastical hierarchies—in Luhmann's framework these are all communicative forms through which the religious system self-reproduces. Luhmann can explain why religious institutions possess self-maintaining inertia—the system continuously reproduces itself through its own operations (autopoiesis).

**Where Luhmann stops.** He stops at two places. First, deep-layer upheaval in xin. Paul's conversion on the road to Damascus—from persecuting Christians to becoming Christianity's most important apostle—is not the self-reproduction of a communicative system but an event internal to a consciousness, and then this event changed the entire system's evolutionary direction. Luhmann's framework classifies such events as "environmental perturbation of the system," but the concept of perturbation is too weak—Paul's event is not a perturbation but a mutation of a core node that directly rewrote Christianity's entire path from an intra-Jewish movement to cross-ethnic propagation. In this framework, this is the deep layer of xin undergoing drastic change in an extremely short time—the direction of semantic flow suddenly shifts (note: the layered structure of xin into deep, middle, and shallow layers appears in the upstream paper's natural language but has not entered the upstream formalization file), with effects propagating through positive feedback loops from individual to entire system.

Second, the death of civilizations. Before the collapse of the Soviet Union, all communicative subsystems were operating normally—the political system, legal system, education system, military system. Under Luhmann's framework, as long as communication continues, the system is self-reproducing. But the Soviet Union died. Luhmann's framework can describe after the fact that "the system's self-reproduction stopped," but struggles to explain why, under conditions where communication was still continuing, the system would stop reproducing. This framework's explanation is: signal containers were intact, but the xin driving the signals had emptied—the gap between semantic flow's direction and signal containers' claims had continuously widened; the positive feedback loop had begun to idle (40a22ff8-9d90-4e1e-82f6-1fd80917c139.Instability). Nobody was genuinely operating with Marxist-Leninist xin anymore; communication had become ritual idling. Signal end normal, semantics side zeroed out—Luhmann does not distinguish these two sides and so cannot see this process.

**Where this framework picks up.** The fundamental divergence between this framework and Luhmann is an architectural choice: he excluded consciousness from the system to gain system-level descriptive power; this framework places consciousness at the center to gain dynamics-level explanatory power. The two are not mutually exclusive—Luhmann sees patterns of communication; this framework sees the driving forces behind the patterns. Applied specifically to religious analysis: Luhmann can describe each of the three religions' communicative structures; this framework can explain why these three sets of communicative structures followed completely different dynamical paths—because the xin driving communication is different, xin's qualitative properties are different, and xin's strategies for handling drift are different.

### **4.4.3 Dawkins: Memetics**
Richard Dawkins' (1976) meme concept is the most widely familiar cultural transmission framework. Memetics' core presupposition is: cultural units can be copied like genes; copying involves variation; variation faces selection pressure; memes with strong adaptability survive. This analogy superficially resembles this paper's evolutionary path.

**Where memetics stops.** It stops at its very foundation. Memetics' basic presupposition—that cultural units can be copied—is directly negated by this framework's postulates. Signals can be copied (Shannon's domain), but semantics cannot. The same Quran is read by one billion people; the signal is identical; the semantics are various. Memetics treats semantics as signals and transformation as a defect of copying—from the starting point it has taken the wrong direction.

Applied to explaining the three paths: memetics can handle only the evolutionary path (variation + selection = evolution) and is nearly helpless with the lockdown and self-organization paths. Islam's lockdown strategy is not "high-fidelity copying of memes"—it is deliberate institutional design aimed at suppressing signal-end variation, but suppressing the signal end does not equal suppressing the semantics side. The Sunni-Shia split can only be explained in memetics as "copying error," but in fact the signal end has no error at all—both sides read the same Quran; the split occurs behind the signal. Buddhism's self-organization path is even more of a blind spot for memetics: skillful means allows the same dharma to be expressed in infinitely many ways; the vastness of the Tripitaka accommodates virtually all variation; there is no mechanism of "selection pressure eliminating unadapted versions." Buddhism does not eliminate; it accommodates. Memetics' "variation + selection" model completely fails for Buddhism.

**Where this framework picks up.** This framework's replacement of memetics is not a correction but a replacement of basic presuppositions. The basic behavior of culture is not copying but transformation. Stability is not the result of high-fidelity copying but the functional convergence of large numbers of transformations on the signal side. The differences among the three paths are not differences in mutation rate but differences in xin's strategy for handling drift—evolution (allowing variation, screening through competition), lockdown (suppressing signal-end variation, squeezing drift to the interpretive end), and self-organization (allowing variation, no adjudication). Memetics has vocabulary for only the first path.

### **4.4.4 Positioning Summary**
The three theories each touch part of this paper's problem. Sperber saw transformation but lacked tools to distinguish the signal side from the semantics side, and lacked "xin" to explain path divergence. Luhmann saw the self-reproduction of systems but excluded consciousness, unable to see how deep upheaval in xin and the idling of positive feedback loops rewrite systems from within. Dawkins saw competition and selection but got the basic presupposition wrong—culture does not copy; culture transforms.

This framework's position is not "it doesn't conflict with them" but rather: the wall each of them hit on the signal or system side, this framework picks up from the other side. Sperber's attractors explain which representations spread easily; this framework explains why they follow different paths after spreading. Luhmann's communicative system describes the institutional form of religion; this framework explains why institutional forms collapse from within or are reorganized from without. Dawkins' selection pressure describes one mechanism of the evolutionary path; this framework explains why there are also lockdown and self-organization—two completely different paths.

Building one's own framework is not the problem; being untestable is. This paper has designated testable predictions for each of the three paths (3.2–3.4), but must honestly admit: these predictions come from the framework's generative case; the framework itself was induced from these observations; using generative-case predictions to verify the framework risks circular reasoning. True testing must come from independent cases—phenomena not considered when the framework was constructed. If someone can use Sperber, Luhmann, or another existing framework to give equally precise explanations of these predictions, this framework is a superfluous hypothesis and should be replaced by a more parsimonious theory. No such alternative has been observed so far, but the absence of alternatives does not equal this framework's correctness.

This paper is a generative case for Semantic Flow Dynamics—the framework grew out of this observation and then turned back to organize it. This is a normal process of theory construction, but it also means this paper cannot serve as verification of the framework. Verification must come from independent cases—phenomena not used during the framework's construction. # **Conclusion**
Religion is the most enduring information flow system in human civilization. Through the five-stage model and three dynamical paths, this paper has demonstrated the operational logic of religion.

The five-stage model is built on structural facts of cognition: values and will precede information reception (xin), the verifiable knowledge domain (inside domain), the boundary of human reason (boundary), the unverifiable domain (outside domain), and the physical termination of consciousness (death). These do not depend on any religious doctrine; they are the structure of cognition itself.

The three religions share the basic elements of amplification—signal transformation, functional convergence, institutionalization of signal containers, and death pressure—but the way the elements combine produces three completely different dynamical paths. Christianity follows the evolutionary path: open channel, unlocked signals, repeated schism and collision, evolving survivors. Islam follows the lockdown path: closed channel, extreme signal lockdown, suppressed variation, steady state. Buddhism follows the self-organization path: no central channel, no lockdown, pure market competition only, no zero-sum game (heresy judgment). All three have competition; the difference is in the structure of competition: the Abrahamic religions have market plus zero-sum; Buddhism has only market.

Path differences are not doctrinal differences—doctrinal differences are surface—but structural differences in information flow. Different paths produce different civilizational forms. Christianity's and Islam's semantics flow back to the signal end, following the externalization path—producing doctrine, law, and institutions; fast expansion but dependent on signal containers. Buddhism's semantics remain on the consciousness end, following the internalization path—entering directly into behavior and lifestyle; slow expansion but extremely resilient. Buddhism is not the area where the framework's explanatory power is weakest but the most extreme case of the postulates: the largest semantic output, the lowest signal reflux rate. "Unsayable" does not mean no semantics; it means semantics too abundant for language to contain.

The unquantifiability of the semantics side is a structural ceiling of human cognition; self-reference necessarily collapses; it applies equally to all theories. This paper chooses to face this ceiling honestly rather than pretending it does not exist. What can be quantified is honestly quantified; what cannot be quantified is honestly acknowledged as such.

This paper's contribution is not in judging the truth of religion but in providing a framework that can analyze religion without discussing doctrine—a framework anchored in structural facts rather than self-constructed definitions. Three Religions, Five Cuts is this analysis's compressed table, the five-stage model is its structural foundation, the three dynamical paths are its core finding, and Semantic Flow Dynamics is its underlying tool.

If religion is an information flow system, then the tool for analyzing it should be the dynamics of information flow—not the positional disputes of theology or atheism. This paper is the first step in that direction—and a generative case for Semantic Flow Dynamics. The framework grew from this observation; this paper used the framework to go back and organize the observation. The next step is independent verification: using the framework to make precise predictions about phenomena not considered during its construction, then seeing whether those predictions hold. Generative cases demonstrate explanatory power; independent verification determines the framework's survival.
